1. Types of Shock 
• Hypovolemic Shock 
• Cardiogenic Shock 
• Distributive Shock: Septic, Anaphylactic, Neurogenic 
• Obstructive Shock 
 
 2. 5 ICU Vital Signs (besides BP, Pulse, RR, Temp, Pain) 
• Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS): Normal = 15 
• Random Blood Sugar (RBS): Fasting 70–120 mg/dL, Postprandial (PP) < 180 mg/dL 
• O2 Saturation: Normal > 96% 
• End-Tidal CO2 (EtCO2): Normal 35–45 mmHg 
• Central Venous Pressure (CVP): Normal 6–8 mmHg 
 
 
3. VAP Bundle 
• Head of bed (HOB) elevation 30–45° 
• Daily sedation vacations + readiness to wean assessment 
• Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) prophylaxis 
• Deep venous thrombosis (DVT) prophylaxis 
• Daily oral care with chlorhexidine 
 
 4. Indications for Mechanical Ventilation (MV) 
• Apnea or absence of breathing 
• Acute ventilatory failure: pH ≤ 7.25 with PaCO2 ≥ 50 mmHg 
• Severe hypoxemia: PaO2 ≤ 50 mmHg on room air 
• Respiratory muscle fatigue 
 
 
5. Causes of Respiratory Failure 
• Airway obstruction 
• Central nervous system (CNS) depression 
• Neuromuscular diseases: Myasthenia gravis (MG), Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS) 
• Chest wall and pleural deformities 
• Pulmonary diseases: Pneumonia, Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), Pulmonary 
edema, Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) 
 
 6. Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) 
• Eye Opening (E): Spontaneous (4), To speech (3), To pain (2), None (1) 
• Verbal Response (V): Oriented (5), Confused (4), Inappropriate words (3), Incomprehensible (2), 
None (1) 
• Motor Response (M): Obeys (6), Localizes (5), Withdrawal (4), Abnormal flexion (3), Extension (2), 
None (1) 
• Total Score: 3 (Deep Coma) → 15 (Normal) 
 
 
7. Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) Types by Occlusion 
• ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI): Complete occlusion 
• Non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI): Partial occlusion with myocardial damage 
• Unstable Angina (UA): Partial occlusion without myocardial damage 
 
 8. Vagotonic Maneuvers to Decrease Heart Rate (HR) 
• Carotid sinus massage 
• Valsalva maneuver 
• Facial immersion in cold water 
• Ocular pressure 
• Self-induced vomiting 
• Breath holding 
• Head lowered between the knees 
 
 
9. Nursing Interventions to Decrease Intracranial Pressure (ICP) 
• Keep head midline and elevate head of bed (HOB) to 30° 
• Avoid hip flexion and neck rotation 
• Space out nursing activities 
• Minimize suctioning (pre-oxygenate if needed) 
• Maintain a quiet and calm environment 
 
 10. Complications of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) 
• Cerebral edema 
• Hypoglycemia (treatment-related) 
• Hypokalemia (treatment-related) 
• Aspiration 
• Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)

11. Treatment of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) 
• Fluid replacement: Normal saline (NS) 
• Electrolyte replacement: Potassium (K+) monitoring and replacement 
• Insulin therapy: Continuous IV insulin infusion 
• Correct acidosis: Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) only if pH < 7.0 
 
 12. Symptoms and Signs of DKA 
• Symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain 
• Signs: Kussmaul respirations, fruity (acetone) breath odor 
• Signs: Dehydration — hypotension, tachycardia 
• Signs: Altered mental status (AMS) 
 
 
13. Stroke Types and Target Blood Pressure (BP) 
• Ischemic stroke: Treat only if BP > 220/120 mmHg 
• Ischemic (thrombolysis candidate): Target BP < 185/110 mmHg before treatment 
• Hemorrhagic stroke: Target systolic blood pressure (SBP) 140–160 mmHg 
 
 14. Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) Diagnosis 
• Clinical: Bleeding from multiple sites (petechiae, ecchymosis, IV sites) 
• Clinical: Signs of microvascular thrombosis leading to organ failure 
• Labs: Prolonged prothrombin time (PT) / partial thromboplastin time (PTT) 
• Labs: Decreased platelets, decreased fibrinogen, elevated D-dimer

15. Causes of Upper Gastrointestinal (GI) Bleeding 
• Peptic ulceration 
• Erosion / mucosal inflammation: Esophagitis, gastritis 
• Esophageal or gastric varices 
• Mallory-Weiss tear 
• Tumors 
 
 16. Precipitants of Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE) 
• Gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding 
• Infections: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) 
• Constipation 
• Dehydration or electrolyte imbalance 
• High protein intake 
• Use of sedatives 
 
 
17. Nursing Interventions for Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE) 
• Administer lactulose to promote ammonia excretion 
• Administer antibiotics: Rifaximin 
• Maintain low-protein diet during acute phases 
• Monitor neurological status frequently 
 
 18. Target Organ Damage in Hypertensive Emergency 
• Central nervous system (CNS): Encephalopathy or intracranial hemorrhage (ICH) 
• Eyes: Grade 3–4 retinopathy or papilledema 
• Heart: Congestive heart failure (CHF), myocardial infarction (MI) 
• Kidneys: Acute kidney injury (AKI) or hematuria 
• Vasculature: Aortic dissection 
 
 
19. Causes of Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) 
• Direct lung injury: Pneumonia, gastric aspiration, lung contusion, drowning 
• Indirect lung injury: Sepsis, severe trauma, massive blood transfusion, acute pancreatitis 
 
 20. Complications of Mechanical Ventilation (MV) 
• Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) 
• Barotrauma / volutrauma (e.g., pneumothorax) 
• Decreased cardiac output (CO) from high intrathoracic pressure 
• Stress ulcers in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract 
 
 
21. Mechanical Ventilation (MV) Alarms 
• High pressure: Coughing, secretions, or biting the tube 
• Low pressure: Disconnection or leaks 
• Low exhaled volume 
• Apnea alarm 
 
 22. Eclampsia 
• Definition: Severe hypertension during pregnancy with seizures 
• Treatment: Volume expansion 
• Treatment: Magnesium sulfate (MgSO4) for seizure prophylaxis and treatment 
• Treatment: Antihypertensives — Hydralazine 
 
 
23. Management of Acute Respiratory Failure 
• Treat the underlying cause 
• Provide oxygen (O2) therapy and maintain airway 
• Restore fluid and electrolyte balance 
• Optimize cardiac function and cardiac output (CO) 
 
 24. Complications of Acute Respiratory Failure 
• Ischemic anoxic encephalopathy 
• Cardiac dysrhythmias 
• Venous thromboembolism (VTE) 
• Gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding 
 
 
25. Differential Diagnosis of Stroke 
• Hypoglycemia 
• Seizures / Post-ictal state 
• Brain tumor or abscess 
 26. Prevention of Stroke 
• Control hypertension (HTN), diabetes mellitus (DM), and hyperlipidemia 
• Lifestyle: Smoking cessation, healthy diet, regular exercise 
• Medication: Antiplatelets or anticoagulants for high-risk patients

• Meningitis or encephalitis 
 
 
27. Management of Stroke 
• Ischemic: Thrombolytics — recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (rtPA) if within time window 
• Ischemic: Antiplatelets — Aspirin; supportive blood pressure (BP) and glucose control 
• Hemorrhagic: Blood pressure (BP) control and intracranial pressure (ICP) management 
• Hemorrhagic: Potential surgical evacuation
