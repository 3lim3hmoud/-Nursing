Acute Renal Failure (ARF): 
Essential Nursing Guide
A concise, clinical primer for nursing students covering causes, pathophysiology, 
assessment, labs, and practical nursing management of acute renal failure (also called 
acute kidney injury).

preencoded.png
What is Acute Renal 
Failure?
Acute Renal Failure is a rapid loss of kidney function causing impaired 
waste excretion, electrolyte dysregulation, and disturbed fluid 
balance. Also called Acute Kidney Injury (AKI).
Incidence
Occurs in ~5% of hospitalized patients—more common in ICU 
settings.
Prognosis
Mortality 7 %–80% depending on severity, comorbidities, and 
timeliness of care.

Causes of ARF — The Three Categories
Prerenal (60–70 %)
Due to reduced renal perfusion: 
dehydration, hemorrhage, heart 
failure, sepsis, shock, medication-
induced vasodilation.
Intrarenal (25 –40%)
Direct kidney damage: acute tubular 
necrosis (ischemia), nephrotoxins 
(aminoglycosides, contrast), 
glomerulonephritis, severe 
rhabdomyolysis.
Postrenal (5–10 %)
Obstruction of urine flow: ureteral 
stones, benign prostatic hypertrophy 
(BPH), strictures, tumors—often 
reversible if relieved early.

Pathophysiology Overview
01
Prerenal Mechanism
Hypoperfusion → decreased 
glomerular filtration rate (GFR). 
Reversible if perfusion restored 
promptly.
02
Intrarenal Mechanism
Tubular cell injury → loss of 
concentrating ability, back-leak 
of filtrate, electrolyte imbalance 
(K+, H+).
03
Postrenal Mechanism
Obstruction → increased tubular pressure → decreased GFR; 
prolonged obstruction causes parenchymal damage.
Note: Untreated prerenal AKI often progresses to intrarenal injury—early detection is 
essential.

The Four Phases of ARF
Initiation
Initial insult — often subtle signs. Goal: identify cause and prevent progression.
Oliguria
Urine output <400 mL/day. Watch for azotemia, hyperkalemia, fluid overload.
Diuresis
Urine output increases; risk of electrolyte loss and dehydration—monitor 
closely.
Recovery
GFR improves gradually; full recovery may take weeks–months; 
residual impairment possible.

Clinical Manifestations Nurses Must Recognize
• Oliguria or anuria; measurable decrease in urine output.
• Fluid overload: peripheral edema, pulmonary congestion, elevated BP.
• Electrolyte disturbances: hyperkalemia (arrhythmia risk), metabolic acidosis.
• Systemic signs: fatigue, confusion, nausea, uremic breath, bleeding tendencies.

Key Laboratory Findings
• Serum creatinine rise: ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hours or ≥1 .5× baseline —
diagnostic criterion.
• Elevated BUN (azotemia) and BUN:Cr ratio shifts depending on cause.
• Electrolytes: hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, 
metabolic acidosis.
• Urinalysis: muddy brown casts (ATN), proteinuria, hematuria, low 
specific gravity if tubular damage.
Nursing implication: rapid reporting of rising K+ or ECG changes is critical.

Nursing Assessment & 
Monitoring
Hourly Urine Monitoring
Record volume, color, clarity; note trends and report <0.5 mL/kg/hr over 6 hours.
Vital Signs & Weights
Daily weights, BP, HR, respiratory status—track fluid balance and overload.
Lab Surveillance
Frequent BUN, creatinine, electrolytes, and serial ECGs if K+ elevated.
Medication Review
Identify nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors with caution, aminoglycosides, 
contrast) and communicate with prescriber.

Nursing Management Priorities
Fluid Management
Balance perfusion vs. 
overload: judicious IV fluids 
for hypovolemia; diuretics 
for volume overload as 
ordered.
Medication Safety
Avoid or dose-adjust 
nephrotoxic agents; 
coordinate with pharmacy 
for alternatives and renal 
dosing.
Nutritional Support
Restrict potassium and 
sodium; moderate protein 
based on stage and dialysis 
status—consult dietitian.
Education & Dialysis 
Prep
Teach symptom reporting, 
fluid restrictions, and 
prepare patients/teams for 
possible renal replacement 
therapy (indications: 
refractory hyperkalemia, 
volume overload, uremia).

Summary & Takeaways for Nurses
Early Recognition
Timely detection of oliguria, rising creatinine, or hyperkalemia alters outcomes—
monitor closely.
Targeted Interventions
Restore perfusion, remove nephrotoxins, correct electrolytes, and support with 
dialysis when indicated.
Collaborative Care
Work with interdisciplinary team—physicians, pharmacists, dietitians—to 
personalize care and prevent complications.
Patient-Centered Role
Nurses are pivotal in monitoring, educating, and advocating—your vigilance 
improves recovery odds.
For quick reference: report urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours, any K+ >5.5 
mEq/L with ECG changes, or rapid creatinine rise immediately.
