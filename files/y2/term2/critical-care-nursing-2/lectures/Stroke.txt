Stroke
• Stroke is the sudden onset of acute neurological deficit persisting for more 
than 24 hours and caused by the interruption of blood flow to a region of 
the brain. 
• Blood flow is disrupted because of an obstruction of a vessel, a thrombus 
or embolus, or the rupture of a vessel. 
• The apparent clinical features depend on the location of the event and 
region of the brain perfused by the vessel.

Classification of Stroke:
• Ischemic Stroke (87% of all strokes)
• Hemorrhagic stroke include:
• Subarachnoid hemorrhages (3% SAHs)
• Intracerebral hemorrhages (10% )

Etiology of Stroke
• Ischemic strokes are due to vascular obstruction (thrombi or emboli), resulting 
in ischemia and infarction.
• Hemorrhagic Stroke are due to:
• Hypertensive vascular disease (which causes an intracerebral 
hemorrhage),
• A ruptured aneurysm is a dilation of the walls of a cerebral artery that 
develops as a result of weakness in the arterial wall.
• A ruptured an Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM) due to an abnormality in 
embryonal development that leads to a tangle of arteries and veins in the 
brain without a capillary bed. 
• The absence of a capillary bed leads to dilation of the arteries and veins and 
eventual rupture.
• They are commonly a cause of hemorrhage in young people.

Risk factors for stroke
• Risk factors include:
• smoking,
• hypertension,
• obesity, 
• cardiac disease, 
• hypercholesterolemia,
• diabetes,
• cancer,

Clinical Manifestations of stroke
• Manifestations depend on the anatomical location of the lesion; an 
infarct in a certain portion of the brain results in loss of function of the 
body part.
• If symptoms resolve in less than 24 hours, the event is classified as a 
Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA),
• is a deficit lasting less than 24 hours that is attributed to focal cerebral 
or retinal ischemia and last for only minutes to less than an hour.

Manifestations of Ischemic Stroke
• Early Sign
• Numbness or weakness of the face, arm, or leg, especially on one side of the 
body
• Confusion or change in mental status
• Trouble speaking or understanding speech
• Visual disturbances
• Difficulty walking, dizziness, or loss of balance or coordination
• Sudden severe headache

Visual Field Deficits
• Homonymous hemianopsia: 
• (loss of half of the visual field) Unaware of persons or objects on side of 
visual loss
• Neglect of one side of the body Difficulty judging distances
• Loss of peripheral vision: 
• Difficulty seeing at night Unaware of objects or the borders of objects
• Diplopia: Double vision.

Motor Deficits
• Hemiparesis: Weakness of the face, arm, and leg on the same side (due 
to a lesion in the opposite hemisphere)
• Hemiplegia: Paralysis of the face, arm, and leg on the same side (due to 
a lesion in the opposite hemisphere)
• Ataxia:
• Staggering, unsteady gait Unable to keep feet together; needs a broad 
base to stand
• Dysarthria: Difficulty in forming words
• Dysphagia: Difficulty in
• swallowing

Sensory  and verbal Deficits
• Paresthesia (occurs on the side opposite the lesion)
• Numbness and tingling of extremity
• Difficulty with proprioception
• Verbal Deficits
• Expressive aphasia: Unable to form words that are understandable; may be 
able to speak in single-word responses
• Receptive aphasia: Unable to comprehend the spoken word; can speak but 
may not make sense
• Global (mixed) aphasia
• :Combination of both receptive and expressive aphasia.

Cognitive and Emotional deficit 
• Cognitive Deficits
• Decreased attention span Impaired ability to concentrate Poor abstract 
reasoning
• Altered judgment
• Emotional Deficits
• Loss of self-control Emotional labiality
• Decreased tolerance to stressful situations
• Depression Withdrawal
• Fear, hostility, and anger Feelings of isolation

Diagnosis of 
Stroke
• Rapid diagnosis of a stroke is essential so that 
appropriate patients can receive thrombolytic 
therapy, the goal of which is to save damaged 
brain tissue and minimize permanent deficits.
• Diagnostic test of stroke includes:
• CT scanning without contrast media is to 
differentiate intracranial hemorrhage from 
ischemic stroke.
• MRI can demonstrate infarction of cerebral tissue 
earlier than CT and MRI determine the type of 
stroke, the size and location of the hematoma, 
and the presence or absence of ventricular blood 
and hydrocephalus.

• CT angiography or magnetic resonance imaging and angiography 
(MRI) of the brain and neck vessels confirm the diagnosis of an 
intracranial aneurysm or AVM. 
• These tests show the location and size of the lesion and provide 
information about the affected arteries, veins, adjoining vessels, 
and vascular branches.
• Other tests includes: 
• transcranial Doppler flow studies; transthoracic or 
transesophageal echocardiography; xenon-enhanced CT scan; 
and Single Photon Emission CT (SPE CT) scan.

• 12-lead electrocardiography, chest 
radiography, and continuous cardiac 
monitoring are suggested to detect a cardiac 
cause or coexisting condition.
• Echocardiography is valuable in identifying a 
cardioembolic phenomenon.
• Laboratory evaluation of hematological 
function, electrolyte and glucose levels, and 
renal and hepatic function is also 
recommended. 
• Arterial blood gas analysis is performed if 
hypoxia is suspected.
• An electro- encephalogram is obtained if 
seizures are suspected.

 Treatment of Ischemic Stroke
• Medical management to restore of Cerebral Blood 
Flow, maintain reperfusion and Prevent recurrent of 
thrombosis. 
•
 Medical treatment includes: 
• thrombolytic therapy with intravenous recombinant 
tissue- type plasminogen activator (rtPA), 
anticoagulant ((IV heparin or low- molecular-weight

• Surgical prevention performing Carotid 
Endarterectomy surgery

Treatment of Hemorrhagic Stroke
•
 Surgical Management 
• to allow the brain to recover from the initial insult (bleeding), to 
prevent or minimize the risk of re-bleeding, and to prevent or treat 
complications. 
•
 The surgical procedure involves:
• A craniotomy to evacuate bleeding or hematoma and expose 
or isolate the area of aneurysm and Arteriovenous 
Malformations (AVM).
• Surgical clipping or endovascular coiling with complete 
obliteration of the aneurysm.

Medical Management of Ischemic Stroke 
Thrombolytic Therapy
• Action of thrombolytic medications is exogenous drugs that 
dissolve clots.
• The t-PA activates plasminogen, a naturally enzyme present in the 
intravascular endothelium that protects against excessive 
clotting.
• Activating plasminogen initiates the process of dissolving the clot 
through fibrinolysis. 
• The maximal time is now expanded from 3 to 4.5 hours or less 
from the onset of the neurological symptoms.

Dose and Route:
• The recommended dose of t-PA is 0.9 mg/kg up to a maximum 
dose of 90 mg.
• The 10% of the total dose as a bolus over the total dose is 
administered as an initial intravenous bolus over one to two 
minutes, and the remaining 90% is administered by intravenous 
infusion over 60 minutes.
• No other antithrombotic therapy should be given for the next 24 
hours. 
• A major risk of this therapy is intracerebral hemorrhage.

Inclusion Criteria of thrombolytic therapy
• Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke
• Time of onset of stroke known and is 3 hours or less
• Systolic blood pressure ≤185 mm Hg; diastolic ≤110 mm Hg
• Not a minor stroke or rapidly resolving stroke
• No seizure at the onset of stroke
• Not taking warfarin (Coumadin)
• Prothrombin time ≤15 seconds or INR ≤1.7

Inclusion Criteria of thrombolytic therapy
• Not receiving heparin during the past 48 hours with elevated partial 
thromboplastin time
• Platelet count ≥100.000/mm3
• No prior intracranial hemorrhage, neoplasm, arteriovenous malformation, 
or aneurysm
• No major surgical procedures within 14 days
• No stroke, serious head injury, or intracranial surgery within 3 months
• No gastrointestinal or urinary bleeding within 21 days

• Side effects:
• Bleeding and hypotension is the most common side effect of t-PA 
administration. 
• Intracranial bleeding is a major complication that occurred in 
approximately 6.4% of patients.
• Nursing Consideration
• Vital sign are obtained every 15 minutes for the first 2 hours, every 30 
minutes for the next 6 hours, then every hour for 16 hours.
• Measure blood pressure, systolic should be less than 180 mm Hg and 
diastolic less than 100 mm Hg.
• Measure arterial blood gases

The patient is closely monitored 
for any bleeding (IV insertion 
sites, urinary catheter site, 
endotracheal tube, nasogastric 
tube, urine, stool, emesis, other 
secretions).
A 24-hour delay in placement of 
nasogastric tubes, urinary 
catheters, and intra-arterial 
pressure catheters is 
recommended.

• Secondary treatment options for
stroke include anticoagulation with
antithrombotic and anti-platelet

• If a patient experiences atrial

• For safety patients should be instructed to obtain medical

Antiplatelet drugs
• These drugs prevent platelets from adhering to 
the wall of an injured blood vessel or other 
platelets and to prevent a future thrombotic or 
embolic event.
• Dipyridamole increases the effect of specific 
factors that act as anti aggregates to reduce 
platelet aggregation.
• Ticlopidine inhibits platelet function by 
suppressing adenosine diphosphate induced 
platelet aggregation and aggregation due to other 
factors.
• The recommended dose of ticlopidine is 250 mg 
twice a day. Neutropenia and 
• thrombocytopenia are known serious adverse 
effects, so it is now rarely used.

• Clopidogrel
• also inhibits the activity of adenosine diphosphate 
but is not associated with an increased risk for 
neutropenia.
• Aspirin
• limits platelet adhesion and aggregation.
• The suggested dose of aspirin is 81 to 325 mg/d.

Surgical Management of Ischemic Stroke
• Carotid endarterectomy Surgery
• Is the removal of an atherosclerotic plaque or thrombus from the 
carotid artery to prevent stroke in patients with occlusive disease 
of the extracranial cerebral arteries.
• This surgery is indicated for patients with symptoms of TIA or mild 
stroke found to be caused by severe (70% to 99%) carotid artery 
stenosis or moderate (50% to 69%) stenosis with other significant 
risk factors.

Complications Post Endarterectomy
• Incision hematoma: 
• Occurs in 5.5% of patients. Large or rapidly expanding hematomas 
require emergency treatment.
• If the airway is obstructed by the hematoma, the incision may be 
opened at the bedside.
• Hypertension: 
• Poorly controlled hypertension increases the risk of postoperative  
complications,  including  hematoma  and  hyperperfusion.

• syndrome. There is an increased incidence of neurologic 
impairment and death due to intracerebral hemorrhage.
• Postoperative hypotension:
• Occurs in approximately 5% of patients, treated with fluids and 
low-dose phenylephrine infusion. 
• Usually resolves in 24–48 h. Patients with hypotension should 
have serial ECGs to rule out myocardial infarction.

Hyperperfusion Syndrome:
• Occurs when cerebral vessel autoregulation fails.
• Arteries adapted to diminished blood flow may be permanently 
dilated; increased blood flow after endarterectomy attached with 
insufficient vasoconstriction leads to capillary bed damage, 
edema, and hemorrhage.
