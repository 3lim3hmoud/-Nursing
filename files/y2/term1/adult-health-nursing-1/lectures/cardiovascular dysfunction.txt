Definition: 
Rheumatic fever is an inflammatory 
disease that can develop as a 
complication of inadequately treated 
strep throat which caused by an 
infection with group A streptococcus 
bacteria.

Incidence  
 
 Rheumatic fever occurs as an 
autoimmune  reaction to an upper 
respiratory (throat) streptococci 
infection . 
 
 From 2:4 weeks after the streptococcal 
infection.

Patient with recurrent infection (sore 
throat  and  tonsillitis). 
 It typically occurs between ages 5 and 15 
 Malnutrition, overcrowding 
 low socioeconomic status.

"Jones Criteria  
 
2 major criteria  
or  
1 major + 2 minor criteria.

Minor criteria  Major criteria  
Fever  Carditis 
Joint pain Arthritis  
Abnormalities of ECG  Chorea 
Presence of certain 
protein in blood  
 Subcutaneous nodules 
 Erythema marginatum

Diagnostic assessment: - 
 
❖ Positive throat culture for streptococci. 
❖ Increase in cardiac enzymes.  
❖ Positive C-reactive protein, ESR which is 
elevated in inflammation 
❖ Increase in WBC  
❖ Echocardiogram to assess for damage to valves

Treatment 
:• Administer non steroidal anti-inflammatory medication 
(Aspirin) 
• Administer antibiotics (Erythromycin, penicillin)  
•If damage of heart valves  occur,  valve replacement is 
required 
• Anticoagulation if atrial fibrillation.

Nursing Intervention 
➢ Monitor for difficulty breathing (dyspnea) ,hacking, 
nonproductive cough, as these are signs of heart failure. 
➢ Determine if patient is allergic to penicillin. 
➢ Monitor S&S of infection because RF may recur: 
• Red, sore throat with pain when swallowing. 
• Swollen cervical lymph glands. 
• Headache. , Temperature greater than 100F (37.8 OC)

Explain to patient: 
• Anticoagulation use 
need for frequent lab monitoring. 
• Avoid contact with people has a RTI 
• Maintain good dental hygiene. 
• Check  the physician, if detect signs of heart failure: 
shortness of breath, weight gain, nonproductive cough. 
• Return to normal activities slowly

Definition  
  Any dysfunction or abnormality of one or 
more of the heart's valves . The valves may become 
Stenotic or Regurgitant.  
 
 Symptoms depend on the specific valve 
involved and the severity of dysfunction

General Symptoms 
• Shortness of breath  especially during exertion or 
when lying flat (orthopnea). 
• Fatigue and weakness 
• Palpitations 
• Chest pain or discomfort 
• Dizziness or syncope (fainting) 
• Swelling in ankles or abdomen (edema) 
• Rapid weight gain due to fluid retention

Risk factors 
• Age > 65 years 
• History of rheumatic fever 
• Infective endocarditis 
• High blood pressure 
• Coronary artery disease 
• Congenital heart defects 
• Autoimmune disorders 
• Radiation to the chest 
• Intravenous drug use (risk of infective 
endocarditis) 
• Hyperlipidemia, diabetes, smoking

Causes  
Congenital  
Acquired  
• Rheumatic fever : (especially mitral valve) 
• Age-related degeneration – calcification. 
•  Endocarditis : bacterial infection damaging the 
valves 
• Ischemic heart disease (IHD). 
• Radiation therapy to the chest 
• Autoimmune diseases (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis) 
• Cardiomyopathy.  
• Congenital heart defects , 
malformed valves at birth

Diagnosis of Valvular Heart Disease 
Physical examination:  
 Presence of symptoms. 
  Murmurs on auscultation  
 Signs of heart failure or fluid overload. 
Diagnostic studies : 
 Echocardiograph 
 Electrocardiogram (ECG 
 Chest X-ray 
 Cardiac Catheterization 
 Cardiac MRI 
 Cardiac CT

• Treat strep throat  
•  Good dental hygiene (reduces endocarditis risk) 
• Prophylactic antibiotics before certain procedures 
(for high-risk individuals) 
• Healthy lifestyle: exercise, balanced diet, avoid 
smoking and alcohol 
• Control risk factors: manage hypertension, 
diabetes, and cholesterol 
• Regular cardiac checkups (especially if there’s a 
murmur or family history).

Treatment

• Beta-blockers  
• Anticoagulants Medical

3- Management of patient with 
Arteriosclerosis and 
Atherosclerosis

• Atherosclerosis is a chronic condition 
characterized by the buildup of fatty deposits 
(plaques) inside the walls of arteries, leading to 
thickening, hardening, and loss of elasticity of 
the arterial walls.

This process narrows the arteries, restricting 
blood flow and potentially causing 
cardiovascular diseases such as coronary 
artery disease and stroke.
