Fluid volume deficit 
 
Fluid output exceeds the fluid intake 
Causes:  
 Gastrointestinal losses: vomiting, nasogastric suctioning. 
Diarrhea. 
 Skin losses: diaphoresis. 
 Renal losses: diuretic therapy, diabetes insipidus. 
 Hemorrhage. 
 Altered intake: such as nothing by mouth (NPO). 
 Frequent enemas

Fluid volume deficit 
 
Clinical manifestation: 
 Vital signs: hyperthermia, thready pulse, hypotension, 
tachypnea, and tachycardia 
 Neuromusculoskeletal: Dizziness, syncope, confusion, 
weakness, fatigue 
 Gastrointestinal: Thirst, dry tongue, nausea, vomiting, 
anorexia, weight loss, and constipation  
 Renal: Oliguria 
 Poor skin turgor, dry skin, and dry mucous membranes

Fluid volume deficit 
 
Complications: 
 Hypovolemic Shock (is a life-threatening emergency) 
 Death

Fluid volume deficit 
Nursing management  
Administer oxygen. 
Provide fluid replacement : oral if conscious and 
parenteral (whole blood, packed RBCs, plasma, ringer , 
lactate, normal saline) 
Administer vasoconstrictors, such as dopamine and 
norepinephrine; coronary vasodilators.

Fluid volume deficit 
Nursing management  
Assess skin and mucous membranes for dryness 
Assess capillary refill appropriately. 
Monitor intake and output. 
Monitor serum electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, urine electrolytes 
Monitor cardiac rhythm if there is an electrolyte disturbance.

Fluid volume excesses  
Excessive retention of water & sodium  
Causes:  
 Renal failure  
 
 Excessive sodium intake. 
 
 
 Age-related changes in cardiovascular and renal 
function.

Fluid volume excesses  
 
Clinical manifestation: 
 Vital signs: hypertension, tachypnea, tachycardia, 
Increased central venous pressure. 
 Neuromusculoskeletal: confusion, muscle weakness, 
headache 
 Respiratory: dyspnea, orthopnea, crackles  
 Edema, distended neck veins, pale and cool skin.

Fluid volume excesses 
Nursing management  
• Monitor intake and output. 
• Administer oxygen as prescribed. 
• Reduce IV flow rates. 
• Administer diuretics (osmotic, loop) as prescribed. 
• Limit fluid and sodium intake as prescribed. 
• Reposition the client at least every 2 hr. 
• Monitor vital signs and heart rhythm. 
• Encourage clients to weigh themselves daily.

Sodium  Imbalance 
          Hyponatremia                     Hypernatremia  
Hyponatremia occurs when the sodium level is less than 135 mEq/L.  
Causes: 
1. GIT loss: as vomiting, diarrhea 
2. Na loss in urine: with renal failure, diuretics 
3. Blood loss. 
4. Hypovolemia & excessive diaphoresis. 
5. Restricted dietary intake (Restricted Na+ in diet).

Clinical manifestation of hyponatremia 
 Vital signs: hypotension, tachypnea, tachycardia, 
hypovolemia. 
 Oliguria 
 Dry skin & lax S.C tissue. 
 Weakness, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea may also 
occur due to increased gastrointestinal motility. 
 Sunken eyes

Nursing management  
• Monitor intake and output. 
• Monitor vital signs and heart rhythm. 
• Monitor LOC, 
• Encourage clients to weigh themselves daily. 
• Monitor for signs of dehydration: decreased skin turgor, dry 
mucous membranes, decreased sweating.  
• Increase sodium in diet appropriately

Hypernatremia occurs when the sodium level is above 145 
mEq/L.  
 
Causes: 
• Excessive saline transfusion in the early post-
operative period 
• Na+ resorption with (Aldosterone or cortisone) 
• Inability to excrete Na+. 
• Abnormal renal retention of Na+.

Clinical manifestation of hypernatremia 
 Slight puffiness of the face  
  Edema  
  Ascites. 
 Weight gain due to fluid retention 
 Hypertension 
 Mental status changes, such as agitation, 
confusion, and personality changes.

Nursing management  
• Report abnormal laboratory findings to the 
provider. 
• Monitor level of consciousness and ensure 
safety. 
• Monitor vital signs and heart rhythm. 
• Provide oral hygiene and other comfort 
measures to decrease thirst. 
• Monitor intake and output 
• Weigh daily and compare

Potassium Imbalance 
           Hypokalemia                  Hyperkalemia 
Hypokalemia  
occurs when the serum potassium level below 3.5 meq/l  
Causes: 
1. GIT loss: as vomiting, diarrhea. 
2. Certain types of medications (lasix) 
3. Major surgery and hemorrhage. 
4. Excessive diaphoresis. 
5. Restricted dietary intake (rare).

Clinical manifestation of hypernatremia 
 Most patients are asymptomatic. 
  Malaise and weakness. 
 The motility of the GI system is slowed, causing 
nausea, vomiting, abdominal distention, and 
constipation. 
 Muscle cramping

Irregular heartbeat (dysrhythmia), which can lead to 
a cardiac arrest 
 
ECG changes:  depression of the ST segment, and 
inversion of the T wave.

Medical treatment: 
 Oral potassium supplements are given. 
 Correct fluid imbalance. 
 Monitor cardiac rhythm. 
 Stop or change medications that contribute to 
potassium loss, if possible (as diuretics). 
 Encourage potassium-rich foods. 
 Administer potassium supplements. 
 Administer potassium in intravenous fluid

Potassium is never given by IV push 
: because; Hyperkaliemia may cause 
cardiac arrhythmias .Potassium is very 
irritant to the vein causing 
thrombophlebitis.

 Precautions for patients receiving oral 
potassium  
 Given in juice or other desired liquids to 
improve taste and to prevent gastrointestinal 
irritation. 
 Take it with meals. 
 Report adverse effects, such as nausea, 
vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal cramping

Nursing management  
• Monitor cardiac system for rate, rhythm 
• Monitor vital signs and heart rhythm. 
• Monitor intake and output 
• Monitor intravenous site for redness, 
swelling, warmth, and pain. 
• Monitor level of consciousness 
• Provide oral potassium 
supplementation.

Foods rich in potassium (bananas, 
tomatoes, orange juice ,avocados, 
broccoli, cantaloupe.)

Hyperkalemia  
occurs when the serum potassium level above  5 meq/l  
 
Causes: 
1. Overuse of potassium. 
2. Excessive intake of oral or IV potassium supplements 
3. Patients with renal failure are at risk for hyperkalemia 
because the kidneys cannot excrete potassium.

Clinical manifestation of hyperkalemia 
 Muscle twitches and cramps, followed by muscular 
weakness. 
 Increased GI motility; diarrhea, Nausea, vomiting. 
 Abdominal distention. 
 Slow, irregular heart rate, and decreased blood 
pressure. 
 Weakness and dizziness
