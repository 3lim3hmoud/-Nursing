Introduction 
• The ECG detects a summation of electrical signals generated by specialized 
• cells of the heart called pacemaker cells.
• Pacemaker cells have the property of automaticity meaning these cells can 
generate a stimulus or an action potential without outside stimulation. 
• This electrical signal is conducted through specialized fibers of conduction 
system to mechanical or muscle cells of the heart where a cardiac 
contraction is generated

Cardiac conduction system: 
•-SA node (Sinoatrial) node 
•-A V node (Atrioventricular) node 
•-Bundle of His 
•-Purkinje Fibers

Waveforms and Intervals
• ECG paper records the speed and magnitude of electrical 
impulses on a grid composed of small and large boxes. 
Every large box has five small boxes in it. 
• At a standard paper speed of 25 mm/sec, on the horizontal 
axis, one small box (1 mm) is equivalent to 0.04 second, 
and one large box (5 mm) represents 0.20 second.

During the cardiac cycle, the 
following waveforms and intervals 
are produced on the ECG surface 
tracing.
• P, ORS, and T wave, these 
waveforms rise from a flat base 
line called isoelectric line.

• The P wave represents atrial depolarization, followed 
immediately by atrial systole.
• The QRS represents ventricular depolarization, followed 
immediately by ventricular systole.
• The ST segment corresponds to phase 2 of the action 
potential, during which time the heart muscle is completely 
depolarized and contraction normally occurs. 
• The T wave represents ventricular repolarization.
• The PR interval, measured from the beginning of the P wave to 
the beginning of the QRS,

• P wave
• Characteristic of P wave
• *Location: It normally seen before the 
QRS complex at a consistent interval. 
• *Duration: From 0.06 to less than 0.12 
second. 
• *Configuration: The P wave is a small, 
usually upright and rounded deflection, 
representing depolarization of the atria.

•  The PR interval represents the time from the onset of atrial 
depolarization until the onset of ventricular depolarization. 
• The PR interval is measured from the beginning of the P wave to the 
beginning of the QRS complex. A normal PR interval is 0.12 to 0.20 
second.

The QRS complex is a large waveform 
representing ventricular depolarization.

  Configuration : The initial negative

 Duration: A normal QRS complex

ST segment
•  The ST segment is the portion of the tracing from the 
end of the QRS complex to the beginning of the T wave. 
• It represents the time from the end of ventricular 
depolarization to the beginning of ventricular 
repolarization.  
•  Normally, it is isoelectric. 
• An isoelectric ST segment means that the ST segment 
joins the QRS complex at the baseline.

QT interval 
• The QT interval is measured from the beginning of 
the QRS complex to the end of the T wave and 
indicates the total time interval from the onset of 
depolarization to the completion of repolarization. 
• The faster the heart rate is, the shorter the QT interval. 
Location: 
• extended from the beginning of QRS complex to the 
end of the T wave. 
Duration: 
• varies according to age, sex, and heart rate   ; usually 
lasts from 0.36 to 0.44 second.

U wave 
• U wave is a rarely seen, small, usually positive deflection after the T 
wave. The U  wave represents repolarization of small segment of the 
ventricles. 
•  Large U waves may be present in patient with hypokalemia.

Analyzing a Rhythm Strip
Steps of Analyzing a Rhythm Strip 
• Determine the regularity of the rhythm 
• Regularity is assessed by using calipers or a piece of EKG paper. 
• *To determine atrial regularity, identify   the P wave and place one caliper point on the 
peak of the P wave.
•  Located the next P wave and place the second caliper on its peak. 
• If the first caliper point lands exactly on the next P wave, the atrial rhythm is 
perfectly regular.

• To determine ventricular rhythm regularity measure the intervals 
between two consecutive R wave, if these intervals are equal, the 
rhythm is consider regular.
•  If the rhythm varies by 0.08 seconds (2 small squares), the rhythm 
would still be considered regular  
• Determine the regularity of the rhythm

Calculate the Heart Rate
• Under normal condition, the atria and ventricles depolarize in 
regular sequence. 
• Methods of Calculating the Heart Rate 
• Large Box Method 
• Small Box Method 
• Six –Second Method

Large Box Method: (Regular Rhythm) 
• In this method, two consecutive P waves 
and QRS complexes are located. 
• *The number of large boxes between the 
highest points of consecutive P wave is 
counted0, and the number of large boxes is 
divided into 300 to determine atrial rate.

• *The number of large boxes between the highest points of 
consecutive QRS complexes is counted, and the number of large 
boxes is divided into 300 to determine ventricular rate. 
• Example: If there are 4 large squares between regular QRS 
complexes, the heart rate is 75 (300/4=75) 
Large Box Method: (Regular 
Rhythm)

• The number of small boxes between the 
highest points of consecutive P wave is 
counted, and the number of small boxes is 
divided into 1500 to determine atrial rate. 
•  *The number of small boxes between the 
highest points of consecutive QRS 
complexes is counted, and the number of 
small boxes is divided into 1500 to determine

• Small Box Method: (Regular Rhythm)

Six –Second Method (Irregular Rhythms) 
• Both the atrial and the ventricular rates can be estimated by examining the 
EGG. 
• The ventricular rate is the number of QRS complexes in a 6-second rhythm 
strip. 
• Counting the number of R waves in a 6-second strip and then multiplying 
that number by 10, this yields heart rate for 60 seconds or 1 minute.

3) Evaluate P Wave 
• -Are P waves present? 
• -Do the P waves have a normal configuration? 
• -Does the entire P wave have a similar size and shape? 
• -Is there one P wave for every QRS complex?

4) Determine PR Interval Duration 
• To measure the PR interval, count the small squares 
between the start of P wave and the start of QRS complex; 
then multiply the number of squares by 0.04 second. 
• Normal PR Interval is 0.12- 0.20 seconds 
• After performing this calculation ask these questions: 
•  Does the duration of PR interval within normal limit? 
•  Is the P R interval constant?

5) Determine QRS Complex Duration and shape  
• Count the number of small squares between the beginning 
and the end of QRS complex & multiply this number by 0.04 
second. 
• -Does the duration of the QRS complex fall within normal 
limit? 
• The normal duration of the QRS complex ranges from 0.06 to 
0.10 second. 
• -Are all QRS complex the same size and shape? 
• -Does a QRS complex appears after every P wave?

6) Evaluate the T Wave 
•-Are T waves present? 
•-Do all of the T waves have a normal shape?
