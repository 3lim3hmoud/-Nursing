Lecture 2: Technical Writing 
1. Define technical communication and its primary purpose. 
Answer: 
Technical communication is the process of presenting technical information clearly and 
effectively to specific audiences. 
Its main purpose is to help people understand information and take action or make 
decisions. 
 
2. Explain the six factors of accessibility in technical documents. 
Answer: 
• Accuracy: Information is correct and error-free. 
• Clarity: Meaning is clear with no ambiguity. 
• Completeness: All required information is included. 
• Diction: Appropriate and suitable language is used. 
• Organization: Ideas are arranged logically. 
• Visual effectiveness: Proper layout, design, and graphics. 
 
3. Describe the five stages of the technical writing process. 
Answer: 
1. Planning and researching – gathering information. 
2. Organizing and drafting – arranging ideas and writing the first draft. 
3. Improving style – making writing clear and effective. 
4. Designing the document – using visuals and layout. 
5. Revising and editing – reviewing content and correcting errors. 
 
4. Discuss the rhetorical situation and its main elements. 
Answer: 
The rhetorical situation explains why and how a document is written. 
Its elements include:

• Subject – the topic. 
• Purpose – the goal of the document. 
• Readers – the target audience. 
• Context – where and when the document is used. 
 
5. What are the four levels of revision in technical writing? 
Answer: 
• Revising: Reviewing content and purpose. 
• Substantive editing: Improving organization and design. 
• Copyediting: Correcting grammar and sentence structure. 
• Proofreading: Final check for spelling and punctuation. 
 
Lecture 3: Nurses Report Sheets 
1. What is a nursing report sheet and why is it important? 
Answer: 
A nursing report sheet is a document used to record patient data and care information. 
It is important because it ensures effective communication and continuity of care 
between shifts. 
 
2. List five benefits of nursing report sheets. 
Answer: 
• Improves patient care. 
• Enhances patient safety. 
• Reduces medical errors. 
• Saves time and increases efficiency. 
• Can be customized for departments.

3. Describe the main components of a patient report sheet. 
Answer: 
• Patient identification data. 
• Diagnosis and medical history. 
• Medications and treatments. 
• Vital signs and assessments. 
• Intake and output records. 
• Planned procedures and care plan. 
 
4. Give four tips for completing a report sheet effectively. 
Answer: 
• Use approved abbreviations. 
• Focus on critical information. 
• Update data during the shift. 
• Review accuracy before handover. 
 
Lecture 4: Practical Application 
1. How are medical history and allergies documented in the Sarah Johnson case? 
Answer: 
Her history includes asthma, seasonal allergies, and previous surgery. 
Her penicillin allergy is clearly documented to prevent medication errors. 
 
2. What are the care priorities for a patient with Influenza B? 
Answer: 
• Apply droplet isolation precautions. 
• Encourage adequate fluid intake. 
• Monitor body temperature regularly.

3. Explain the relay runner’s baton analogy in nursing reports. 
Answer: 
The report sheet is like a baton passed between nurses. 
Good handover ensures smooth care, while poor handover risks patient safety. 
 
Lecture 5: Records 
1. Define records and state their importance in healthcare. 
Answer: 
Records are written documents that document actions and events. 
In healthcare, they provide legal evidence, continuity of care, and patient safety. 
 
2. List the characteristics of good records. 
Answer: 
Good records are accurate, complete, updated, organized, confidential, and objective. 
 
3. Compare alphabetical and numerical filing systems. 
Answer: 
• Alphabetical filing: Arranged by names and easy to use. 
• Numerical filing: Arranged by numbers and useful for large systems. 
 
4. Explain the black box analogy of records. 
Answer: 
Like an airplane’s black box, records store complete and permanent information for 
safety and legal accountability. 
 
Lecture 6: The Nursing Process 
1. Define the nursing process and its characteristics. 
Answer: 
The nursing process is a systematic method for planning patient care. 
It is cyclic, dynamic, patient-centered, and collaborative.

2. Differentiate between subjective and objective data. 
Answer: 
• Subjective data: Patient-reported symptoms (e.g., pain). 
• Objective data: Measurable signs (e.g., temperature). 
 
3. Explain the PES format. 
Answer: 
• P: Problem 
• E: Etiology 
• S: Signs and symptoms 
Used to write clear nursing diagnoses. 
 
4. Describe the four types of nursing assessment. 
Answer: 
• Initial assessment 
• Problem-focused assessment 
• Emergency assessment 
• Time-lapsed assessment 
 
Lecture 7: Nursing Reports 
1. Identify the three main types of nursing reports. 
Answer: 
• Shift report 
• Admission report 
• Discharge report 
 
2. What are the goals of a shift report? 
Answer:

• Ensure continuity of care. 
• Reduce errors. 
• Transfer accurate information. 
• Provide legal documentation. 
 
3. List components of a shift report. 
Answer: 
Patient information, current status, recent changes, care plan, medications, IV access, 
discharge planning, and handover details. 
 
Lecture 8: SBAR Technique 
1. What does SBAR stand for and why is it used? 
Answer: 
SBAR stands for Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation. 
It is used to communicate critical information clearly and quickly. 
 
2. How does SBAR improve patient safety? 
Answer: 
It reduces miscommunication, organizes information, and prevents vague messages. 
 
3. Describe the Recommendation step. 
Answer: 
It states what action is needed, the time frame, and confirms orders for accuracy. 
 
4. Explain the mechanic analogy of SBAR. 
Answer: 
• Situation: current problem 
• Background: history 
• Assessment: suspected cause

• Recommendation: required action
