INTRODUCTION
• Angina pectoris is chest discomfort caused by 
transient myocardial ischemia—an imbalance between 
myocardial oxygen supply and oxygen demand.
• It is a clinical syndrome, not a disease itself, and is most 
commonly due to coronary artery disease (CAD).

DEMAND INCREASES 
WITH:
•Heart rate
•Contractility
•Ventricular wall stress (affected by preload and 
afterload)

Myocardial Oxygen Supply
Supply is reduced by:
• Atherosclerotic narrowing of coronary arteries
• Coronary vasospasm
• Hypotension
• Severe anemia or hypoxia
When supply falls relative to demand → ischemia → chest 
pain.

STABLE ANGINA
•Predictable, exertional chest discomfort
•Lasts <10 minutes
•Relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
•Due to fixed atherosclerotic plaque causing ≥70% 
stenosis

UNSTABLE ANGINA (UA)
•New-onset angina, increasing frequency/severity, or 
occurring at rest
•Part of acute coronary syndrome (ACS)
•Usually due to plaque rupture + platelet aggregation
•Not relieved by rest as reliably; requires urgent 
evaluation

PRINZMETAL (VARIANT) 
ANGINA•Caused by coronary vasospasm
•Pain typically occurs at rest, often early morning
•Transient ST-segment elevation during episodes
•Responds to calcium channel blockers and 
nitrates

MICROVASCULAR ANGINA (CARDIAC 
SYNDROME X)
•Ischemic symptoms without major epicardial 
coronary stenosis
•Related to endothelial dysfunction or small-vessel 
disease
•More common in women

TYPICAL SYMPTOMS
•Tightness, pressure, squeezing, “heaviness”
•Substernal pain radiating to:
•Left arm
•Neck, jaw
•Back or epigastrium
•Duration: minutes (not seconds or hours)

PRECIPITATING FACTORS
•Physical exertion
•Emotional stress
•Cold exposure
•Heavy meals

RELIEVING FACTORS
•Rest
•Sublingual nitroglycerin

CLINICAL ASSESSMENT
•Based on history: typical, atypical, or non-
anginal chest pain
•Cardiovascular risk factors: smoking, 
diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, 
family history

ECG
•Often normal at rest
•During pain: ST depression or T-wave 
inversions

LABORATORY TESTS
•Troponins (to rule out myocardial 
infarction)
•Normal in stable angina
•Normal in Prinzmetal angina
•May be slightly elevated in unstable
angina

STRESS TESTING
•Exercise ECG test
•Stress echocardiography or nuclear 
imaging for unclear cases

CORONARY 
ANGIOGRAPHY
•Gold standard for anatomic 
evaluation
•Used in high-risk or treatment-
refractory patients

GOALS OF TREATMENT
•Symptom relief
•Improve quality of life
•Prevent myocardial infarction and death
•Slow progression of atherosclerosis

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS
•Smoking cessation
•Regular exercise (cardiac rehab)
•Weight control
•Low-fat, Mediterranean-style diet
•Control of diabetes and hypertension

IMMEDIATE RELIEF
•Sublingual nitroglycerin

CHRONIC CONTROL
• Beta-blockers — ↓ heart rate and O2 demand
• Calcium channel blockers — vasodilation; especially in vasospastic 
angina
• Long-acting nitrates
• Ranolazine — reduces late Na+ current; used when symptoms persist
• Antiplatelet therapy: Aspirin ± clopidogrel
• Statins — plaque stabilization and lipid lowering
• ACE inhibitors/ARBs — beneficial in CAD, hypertension, diabetes

REVASCULARIZATION
•Percutaneous coronary intervention 
(PCI)
•Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG)
Indicated in multi-vessel disease, left main 
disease, or severe symptoms despite optimal 
medical therapy.

COMPLICATIONS
•Progression to unstable angina or 
myocardial infarction
•Heart failure from chronic ischemia
•Arrhythmias
•Sudden cardiac death

PROGNOSIS
Depends on:
• Severity of CAD
• Left ventricular function
• Control of risk factors
• Adherence to therapy
Stable angina with good management often has an excellent prognosis; 
unstable angina carries a higher risk of MI.

SUMMARY
•Angina pectoris is a symptom of myocardial 
ischemia, 
•most commonly from coronary atherosclerosis. 
•Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by ECG and 
stress testing. 
•Management focuses on symptom control, risk 
reduction, and prevention of MI through lifestyle 
changes, medication, and revascularization when 
necessary.
