Heart Failure 
1. Definition 
Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome in which the heart is unable to pump sufficient 
blood to meet the metabolic needs of the body. 
2. Classification 
A. By Time Course 
- Acute Heart Failure: Sudden onset (e.g., due to MI, acute valve failure). 
- Chronic Heart Failure: Long-standing, progressive. 
 
B. By Side of Heart Involved 
- Left-sided HF: Pulmonary congestion predominates. 
- Right-sided HF: Systemic venous congestion predominates. 
 
C. By Ejection Fraction 
- HFrEF (EF ≤ 40%): Systolic dysfunction (impaired contraction) 
- HFpEF (EF ≥ 50%): Diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation) 
- HFmrEF (EF 41–49%): Mildly reduced EF 
3. Etiology 
Common Causes: 
- Ischemic heart disease (most common) 
- Hypertension 
- Valvular heart disease 
- Cardiomyopathies (dilated, hypertrophic, restrictive) 
- Arrhythmias (AF, tachycardia-induced) 
- Congenital heart disease 
4. Pathophysiology 
1. Decreased cardiac output → reduced tissue perfusion 
2. Neurohormonal activation: 
   - ↑ Sympathetic activity → vasoconstriction, tachycardia 
   - ↑ RAAS → sodium & water retention 
   - ↑ ADH → further fluid retention 
3. Myocardial remodeling → hypertrophy, dilation, worsening HF 
5. Clinical Features 
A. Left-Sided Failure: 
- Dyspnea (especially on exertion) 
- Orthopnea 
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea

- Fatigue, weakness 
- Cough, frothy sputum 
- Basal lung crepitations 
 
B. Right-Sided Failure: 
- Peripheral edema 
- Hepatomegaly 
- Ascites 
- Jugular venous distension (JVD) 
- Weight gain 
6. Investigations 
ECG: LVH, ischemia, arrhythmia 
Chest X-ray: Cardiomegaly, pulmonary congestion 
Echocardiography: EF measurement, structural abnormalities 
BNP / NT-proBNP: Elevated in HF 
Blood tests: Renal function, electrolytes, thyroid function 
Cardiac MRI: Myocardial fibrosis, infiltration (if indicated) 
7. Management 
A. General / Lifestyle: 
- Sodium restriction (<2 g/day) 
- Fluid restriction (in severe cases) 
- Weight monitoring 
- Stop smoking, alcohol 
- Exercise as tolerated 
 
B. Pharmacological: 
ACE inhibitors / ARBs (Enalapril, Losartan): ↓ Afterload, prevent remodeling 
Beta-blockers (Carvedilol, Metoprolol): ↓ Sympathetic drive 
Diuretics (Furosemide): Relieve congestion 
Aldosterone antagonists (Spironolactone): ↓ Mortality 
SGLT2 inhibitors (Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin): Improve outcomes 
Digoxin (in select cases): ↑ Contractility 
 
8. Complications 
- Arrhythmias (AF, VT) 
- Thromboembolism 
- Renal failure 
- Cardiogenic shock 
- Death (if untreated or advanced)

9. Prognosis 
Poor if untreated — 5-year mortality ~50% 
Improved survival with guideline-directed therapy 
10. Nursing Considerations 
- Monitor daily weight and I/O 
- Check for edema, lung sounds 
- Administer medications as prescribed 
- Educate patient on diet, adherence, and signs of worsening HF
