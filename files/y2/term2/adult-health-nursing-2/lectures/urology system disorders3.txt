Chronic renal failure, or ESRD, is a 
progressive, irreversible deterioration in renal 
function in which the body’s ability to maintain 
metabolic and fluid and electrolyte balance fails, 
resulting in uremia or azotemia (retention of urea 
and other nitrogenous wastes in the blood).

Pathophysiology 
As renal function declines, the end products of protein 
metabolism (which are normally excreted in urine) 
accumulate in the blood. Uremia develops and adversely 
affects every system in the body. The greater the buildup of 
waste products, the more severe the symptoms

Preventing Infection
Patients with ESRD commonly have low 
white blood cell counts, these pose a high risk 
for infection. Preventing and controlling 
infection are essential because the incidence of 
infection is high. Infection of the vascular access 
site and pneumonia are common.

Caring For The Catheter Site
Patients receiving CAPD usually know how to care for the catheter site. 
Recommended daily or three-or-four-times-weekly routine. catheter site care is 
typically performed during showering or bathing. The exit site should not be 
submerged in bath water. The most common cleaning method is soap and water; 
liquid soap is recommended. During care, the nurse and patient need to make sure 
that the catheter remains secure to avoid tension and trauma.

Administering Medications
The medications prescribed for any dialysis 
patient must be closely monitored to avoid those 
that are toxic to the kidneys and may threaten 
remaining renal function.

Providing psychological support
Patients undergoing dialysis for a while 
may begin to re-evaluate their status, the 
treatment modality, and the impact of these 
factors on their families and support systems. 
Nurses must provide opportunities for these 
patients to express their feelings and reactions 
and to explore options.

Renal stones, also known as 
nephrolithiasis or urolithiasis, 
refer to the formation of solid 
crystalline mineral materials 
within the kidneys

Etiology and Risk Factors
A. General Risk Factors
• Dehydration (low urine volume)
• Dietary factors (high sodium, or animal protein)
• Genetic predisposition
• Urinary obstruction
• Metabolic disorders

B. Specific Conditions
• Hypercalciuria
• Hyperoxaluria
• Hyperuricosuria
• Cystinuria (genetic)
• Chronic urinary tract infections

Pathophysiology
Stone formation occurs due to supersaturation of 
solutes in urine, leading to crystal growth, and 
aggregation. Normally, urine contains inhibitors 
(like citrate, magnesium) that prevent stone 
formation, but when these are deficient, stones may 
form.

Differential Diagnosis
•Pyelonephritis
•Appendicitis
•Ectopic pregnancy
•Musculoskeletal pain

Complications
• Obstructive uropathy
• Pyelonephritis or urosepsis
• Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
• Recurrent stones

Nursing Diagnoses
Acute pain related to obstruction of urinary tract by 
kidney stones.
Impaired urinary elimination related to obstruction.
Risk for infection related to urinary stasis.
Deficient knowledge related to prevention of 
recurrence.
Risk for fluid volume deficit related to nausea, 
vomiting, and decreased intake.

Planning
Examples:
• Patient will report pain relief within 30 minutes of 
analgesic administration.
• Patient will maintain urine output ≥30 mL/hr.
• Patient will verbalize understanding of dietary and 
fluid changes to prevent recurrence before 
discharge.

Glomerulonephritis is an inflammation of the 
glomeruli—the tiny filters in the kidneys responsible for 
removing excess waste and fluids from the blood.
It can be acute or chronic, and may lead to kidney failure 
if untreated.

Causes
Post-
streptococcal 
infection 
Autoimmune 
diseases
Vasculitis
Diabetic 
nephropathy

Treatment
• Control BP: ACE inhibitors or ARBs
• Diuretics: for edema
• Corticosteroids : in autoimmune forms
• Antibiotics: if caused by bacterial infection
• Dialysis: in severe renal failure
• Diet: low sodium, low protein, and fluid restriction

Nursing 
Diagnoses
Excess fluid volume related to decreased 
glomerular filtration.
Impaired urinary elimination related to 
inflammation of glomeruli.
Risk for infection related to 
immunosuppressive therapy.
Fatigue related to anemia.
Knowledge deficit related to disease 
management.

Planning / Goals
• Patient will maintain normal fluid balance.
• Patient will demonstrate controlled blood 
pressure.
• Patient will have adequate urine output (>30 
mL/hr).
• Patient will verbalize understanding of disease, 
and treatment.

Evaluation
• Patient maintains normal urine output and 
fluid balance.
• BP within target range
• Edema reduced
• Patient understands and adheres to 
treatment plan.

Pyelonephritis
Pyelonephritis is a bacterial infection of the 
kidneys and renal pelvis, usually caused by 
ascending infection from the bladder.
It can be acute or chronic, with the latter 
potentially leading to permanent kidney damage.

Causes
Urinary tract infection 
Urinary obstruction (e.g., renal 
stones, enlarged prostate)
