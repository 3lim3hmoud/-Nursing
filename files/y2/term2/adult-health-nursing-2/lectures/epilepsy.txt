Definition: chronic neurological disorder
characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures due to
abnormal electrical activity in the brain.
A person is generally diagnosed with epilepsy after
experiencing two or more unprovoked seizures at least 24 hours
apart.

Children Birth injury, genetic syndromes, infections, 
developmental disorders
Adults Trauma, brain tumors, stroke, infections
Older 
Adults Stroke, Alzheimer's, brain atrophy

• High fever (febrile seizures in children)
• Sleep deprivation
• Substance abuse or withdrawal
• Brain infections (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis)

symptoms of focal seizures
1)Simple focal 
 Consciousness is preserved
Symptoms:
• Involuntary movements in one part of the body (arm, leg, or face) 
• Tingling or numbness 
• Strange sensations (unusual smell or taste) 
• Sudden emotions ( fear, anxiety) 
• The person remembers everything during the seizure 
2) Complex focal
 Consciousness is affected
Symptoms:
• Staring or blanking out 
• No response to speech or surroundings 
• Repetitive automatic movements (lip smacking, hand rubbing, chewing) 
• Confusion
• Usually no memory of the event

symptoms of generalized seizures
Tonic seizure
• Sudden stiffening of the body
• No rhythmic jerking
• Often causes falls
Atonic seizure
• Sudden loss of muscle tone 
• Body goes limp (“drop attack”) 
• Can cause falls and injuries
Tonic-clonic seizure 
• Tonic phase:
• Sudden stiffening of the whole body 
• Loss of consciousness 
• Falling down 
• Clonic phase:
• Repeated jerking movements of the body 
• Rhythmic muscle contractions 
Myoclonic seizure
• Sudden, brief muscle jerks
• Usually affects arms or upper body
• Very short (seconds)

Assessment and diagnostic studies :
❖Detailed history of seizure: onset, duration, triggers, 
symptoms
❖Neurological examination 
❖EEG: detects abnormal brain activity
❖MRI/CT scan: identify presence of lesions ,tumor or stroke
❖Blood tests: metabolic or infectious causes
❖Lumbar puncture: if CNS infection is suspected

• For refractory epilepsy (not controlled by medications)
• Temporal lobectomy
• Corpus callosotomy
• Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS)
• Responsive neurostimulation (RNS)

Nursing management :
Pre-ictal phase (Prodromal / Aura phase)
•
  Before the seizure or early warning signs
• Observe for early warning signs (aura, behavior 
changes, headache, anxiety) 
• Ensure patient safety (prepare environment)
• Keep the patient in a safe area 
• Raise bed rails 
• Remove sharp or dangerous objects 
• Reduce stress and provide reassurance 
• Ensure antiepileptic medications are taken on time 
• Document any aura or pre-seizure symptoms

Nursing management :
Ictal phase 
 During the seizure
• Turn the patient to a lateral (side-lying) position if possible 
• Protect the head with padding 
• Remove nearby harmful objects 
• Loosen tight clothing, especially around the neck 
• Do NOT put anything in the mouth 
• Do NOT try to stop the movements 
• Do NOT restrain
• Monitor: 
• Duration of seizure 
• Type of movements 
• Breathing and level of consciousness 
• Provide oxygen if needed 
• Prepare emergency medications if seizure is prolonged (e.g., 
benzodiazepines as prescribed)

Nursing management :
 Postictal phase (After seizure)
 Recovery phase after the seizure
• Maintain airway patency 
• Keep patient in side-lying position if drowsy 
• Monitor vital signs (BP , pulse, oxygen saturation) 
• Assess level of consciousness gradually 
• Provide a calm and quiet environment 
• Manage symptoms: 
• Fatigue 
• Headache 
• Confusion 
• Document full seizure details: 
• Time of onset and duration 
• Type of seizure 
• Patient response 
• Provide emotional support after recovery
