inflammatory disease of 
the airways that causes

The most

chronic

ENGAGING THE AUDIENCE
➢ Allergy is the strongest predisposing factor for
asthma.
➢ Chronic exposure to airway irritants or allergens
also increases the risk of asthma.
➢ Common allergens can be seasonal (grass, tree, and
weed pollens) or perennial (eg, dust, animal
dander).

Common triggers for asthma 
symptoms and exacerbations include 
airway irritants (e.g., air pollutants, 
cold, heat, weather changes, strong 
odors or perfumes, smoke).
Most people who have asthma are 
sensitive to a variety of triggers.

The underlying pathology in asthma is reversible 
and diffuse airway inflammation that leads to 
airway narrowing. 
When activated, mast cells release several 
chemicals called mediators. These chemicals, 
which include histamine, prostaglandins, and 
leukotrienes, perpetuate the inflammatory 
response
causing increased blood flow, vasoconstriction, 
and, attraction of white blood cells to the area, 
and bronchoconstriction.

Clinical Manifestation
Cough Dyspnea 
Wheezing

Assessment and Diagnostic Findings
• The clinician must determine that episodic symptoms of airflow obstruction are 
present.
• A positive family history and environmental factors, including climate changes 
(particularly cold air), and air pollution, are primarily associated with asthma. 
• Comorbid conditions that may accompany asthma include gastro-esophageal 
reflux disease (GERD), and drug-induced asthma.

sputum and blood tests 
Serum levels of IgE may be elevated if allergy is present
Arterial blood gas analysis and pulse oximetry

Complications
Respiratory failure Pneumonia Atelectasis

Pharmacologic Therapy
1. Quick Relief Medications
• Short acting: - beta2 adrenergic agonists (Ventolin) 
are the medications of choice for relief of acute 
symptoms.
• Anticholinergics (e.g. Atrovent) may be used in 
patients who do not tolerate short acting beta2 
adrenergic agonists.

2. Long-Acting Control Medications
• Corticosteroids are the most effective 
anti-inflammatory medications 
currently available. Initially, an 
inhaled form is used. Patients  should 
rinse their mouth after administration 
to prevent thrush, a common 
complication associated with use of 
inhaled corticosteroids.

Ø Long acting beta2 adrenergic 
agonists are used with anti-
inflammatory medications to 
control asthma symptoms, 
particularly those that occur 
during the night.
o Salmeterol (Serevent) has 
duration of bronchodilator for 
at least 12 hours. It used with 
other medications in long 
term control of asthma.

Management of Exacerbations
✓ Asthma exacerbations are best 
managed by early treatment and 
education, including education 
patients about self-management 
techniques, especially those with 
moderate or severe persistent 
asthma or with a history of severe 
exacerbations.

Oxygen supplementation may be 
required to relieve hypoxemia 
Antibiotics may be appropriate in 
patients with comorbid conditions 
(eg, fever and purulent sputum)

Peak Flow Monitoring
1- Daily peak flow monitoring is recommended 
for patients who meet one or more of the 
following criteria: 
• Have moderate or severe persistent asthma
• Worsening symptoms
2- The patient is instructed in the proper 
technique particularly about using maximal effort

Nursing Management
The immediate nursing care of 
patients with asthma depends on 
the severity of symptoms.
The patient may be treated 
successfully as an outpatient if 
asthma symptoms are relatively 
mild or may require 
hospitalization and intensive care 
if symptoms are acute and severe

The patient and family are often frightened and 
anxious because of the patient’s dyspnea. Therefore, a 
calm approach is an important aspect of care. 
The nurse assesses the patient’s respiratory status 
by monitoring the severity of symptoms, breath 
sounds, pulse oximetry, and vital signs.
Obtains a history of allergic reactions to 
medications before administering medications

❑Administers fluids if the patient is dehydrated
❑If the patient requires intubation, the nurse assists with the 
intubation procedure, continues close monitoring of the 
patient
