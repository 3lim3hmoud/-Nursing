Definition of Pain  
• Pain is described as an unpleasant sensory and 
emotional experience associated with actual or potential 
tissue damage.

Types of Pain 
Acute Pain 
Chronic Pain 
Nociceptive Pain 
Neuropathic Pain

Acute Pain 
• Sudden onset 
• Acute pain has a short duration 
• Acute pain usually corresponds to the healing process 
(30 days), but should not exceed 6 months. 
• It implies tissue damage that is usually from an 
identifiable cause.  
• If under treated, acute pain may bring a prolonged 
stress response and lead to permanent damage to the 
patient’s nervous system.

Chronic Pain 
• It develops when the healing process is incomplete or 
when acute pain is poorly managed 
• Long term (more than 6 months), after the healing 
process from the original injury.  
• It may or may not be associated with an illness.   
• Both acute and chronic pain can have a nociceptive or 
neuropathic origin.

• Nociceptive Pain 
• *Nociceptive pain arises from activation of nociceptors   
• *It can be somatic or visceral.  
• Somatic pain involves superficial tissues, such as the 
skin, muscles, joints, and bones. Its location is well 
defined
• Visceral pain involves organs such as the heart, 
stomach, and liver.

Neuropathic Pain 
• Neuropathic pain arises from a lesion or disease 
affecting the somatosensory system  
• The origin of neuropathic pain may be peripheral or 
central.  
• Neuralgia and neuropathy are examples related to 
peripheral neuropathic pain treatments)

Central neuropathic pain
•  involves the central somatosensory cortex and can be 
experienced by patients after a cerebral stroke.  
• Neuropathic pain can be difficult to manage and frequently 
requires a multimodal approach
•  (i.e., the combinations of several pharmacologic and/none 
pharmacologic

Physiology of Pain 
• Four processes are involved in nociception: 
• Transduction (stimulus triggered)  
• Transmission (the pain impulse moves from the spinal 
cord to the brain) 
• Perception (represent the subjective interpretation of pain) 
• Modulation (neurons from brain stem release neuro 
transmitters that block the pain impulse.

Pain Assessment  
– Pain assessment is an integral part of nursing care. It is a prerequisite 
for adequate pain control and relief. 
– Because pain is recognized as a subjective experience, the patient’s 
self report is considered the most valid measure for pain.  
– Unfortunately in critical care, many factors such as the administration 
of sedative agents, the use of mechanical ventilation, and altered 
levels of consciousness may impact communication with patients.

– Many mechanically ventilated patients can communicate that 
they are in pain by using head nodding, hand motions or by 
seeking attention with other movements. 
– In such a situation, use of appropriate communication 
methods may reduce patients’ distress associated with the 
presence of the endotracheal tube by enabling them to 
report the presence of pain or discomfort in a 
comprehensive way.

The Major Components of Pain Assessment: 
• Non observable or subjective  
• Observable or objective  
• The Subjective Component  
• Pain is known as a subjective experience.
•  The subjective component of pain assessment refers to 
the patient’s self-report about his/her sensorial, affective, 
and cognitive experience of pain.

• *Mechanical ventilation should 
not be a barrier for nurses to 
document patients’ self-reports 
of pain.
•  Many mechanically ventilated 
patients can communicate that 
they have pain or can use pain 
scales by pointing to numbers 
or symbols on the scale.

• The patient’s self-report of pain can also obtained 
by questioning the patient using the mnemonic 
PQRSTU 
• P: provocative and palliative or aggravating factors  
• Q: quality  
• R: region or location, radiation  
• S: severity and other symptoms  
• T: timing  
• U: understanding

The Observable  or Objective Component of Pain 
Assessment 
• When the patient’s self-report is impossible to obtain, nurses can 
rely on the observation of behavioral indicators.
•  Patients who experienced pain during nociceptive procedures 
were three times more likely to have increased behavioral 
responses such as facial expressions, muscle rigidity, and 
vocalization than patients without pain.

Pain Management 
The control of pain can be 
pharmacologic, non pharmacologic
pharmacological pain management 
is divided into three categories of action: opioid 
agonists,  nonopioids,     and adjuvants. 
1-Opioid Analgesics 
The opioids most commonly used and recommended as 
first line analgesics are the agonists.
These opioids bind to mu receptors (transmission 
process) which appear to be responsible for pain relief.  
Opioids should be used as first line therapy 
for treatment of non-neuropathic pain.

Morphine  
Morphine is the most commonly prescribed opioid in 
the critical care unit.
Because of its water solubility, morphine has a 
slower onset of action and a longer duration 
compared with the lipid-soluble opioids (e.g., 
fentanyl).  
Morphine has two main metabolites:  
morphine-3-glucuronide (M3G, inactive) and 
morphine-6-glucuronide (M6G, active). 
Morphine is indicated for severe pain.

• Side Effects of Morphine 
• The hypotensive effect can be 
particularly problematic in the 
hypovolemic patient.  
• The respiratory depressant effect is 
the most serious side effect which 
requiring thorough monitoring.  
• Opioids may stimulate histamine 
release from mast cells, resulting in 
cardiac instability and allergic 
reactions.
• Dosage 
• 1-4 mg IV bolus or 1-10 mg IV 
infusion.

Fentanyl 
Fentanyl is a synthetic opioid preferred for critically ill 
patients with hemodynamic instability or morphine 
allergy.
It is a lipid-soluble agent that has a more rapid onset 
than morphine and a shorter duration.  
Transdermal delivery requires 12 to 16 hours for 
onset of action, and it has duration of 72 hours.

• Fentanyl is available in intravenous, intraspinal, and 
transdermal forms. 
• The transdermal form is commonly referred to as the 
Duragesic patch or the 72-hour patch.
•  When fentanyl is given by rapid administration and at 
higher doses, it has been associated with the additional 
hazard of bradycardia and rigidity in the chest wall 
muscles. 
• Dosage 
• 25-100 mcg IV bolus 
• 25-200 mcg IV infusion

• Hydromorphone 
• Analgesia, anti anxiety  
•  Dosage 
• 0.2 -1 mg IV bolus 
• 0.2 -2 mg IV infusion  
• Side Effect of Hydromorphone:  
• Hydromorphone has the same side effects as 
Morphine.

• Meperidine (Demerol)  
• It is considered the weakest of the opioids, and it must 
be administered in large doses to be equivalent in 
action to Morphine. 
• A major concern with this medication, which is a Center 
Nervous System (CNS) neurotoxic agent.  
• At high doses in patients with kidney failure or liver 
dysfunction or in older adult patients, it may induce 
CNS toxicity, including irritability, muscle spasticity, 
tremors, agitation, and seizures.

Codeine 
Codeine has limited use in the management of severe pain.
It is rarely used in the critical care unit.
It provides analgesia for mild to moderate pain. 
available only through oral, intramuscular, and subcutaneous routes, 
Dosage:15 - 20 mg IM, SC 
Side Effects of Codeine:
the most common side effect of Codeine are lightheadedness, dizziness, 
shortness of breath, sedation, nausea, and vomiting.

Methadone 
Methadone is a synthetic opioid with morphine- like 
properties but less sedation 
Dosage: 5-10 mg IV. The maximum dose is 4 g/day. 
Prolongation of the QT interval, which can lead to 
torsades de pointes, has been reported with its use.

Opioid Reversal:
Critical respiratory depression can he readily 
reversed with the administration of the opiate 
antagonist Naloxone. The usual dose is 0.4 mg, which is mixed with 10 mL 
of normal saline 
Naloxone can be discontinued as soon as the patient 
is responsive to physical stimulation and able to take 
deep breaths.

2-Nonopioid Analgesics 
Acetaminophen 
It is an analgesic used to treat mild to moderate pain. 
It inhibits the synthesis of neurotransmitter prostaglandins in the CNS, 
and this is why it has no anti-inflammatory properties.  
Dosage: 650 mg. the maximum dose is 4 g/day 
Analgesic, Antipyretic 
acetaminophen total dose should not exceed 2 
g/day. Side Effect: hepatotoxicity

Non Steroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs:  
The use of NSAIDs in combination with opioids is 
indicated in the patient with acute musculoskeletal 
and soft tissue inflammation.The mechanism of action of NSAIDs is to block the 
action of cyclooxygenase (COX, which has two 
forms: COX- I and COX-2), the enzyme that converts 
arachidonic acid to prostaglandins.
This inhibits the production of prostaglandins.
