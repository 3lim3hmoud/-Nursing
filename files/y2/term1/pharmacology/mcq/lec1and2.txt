Part II: Essay Model Answers (Summary) 
1. Factors: Drug characteristics, emergency vs. routine use, site of action 
(local/systemic), patient condition (unconscious/vomiting), age, and first-pass 
metabolism. 
2. Comparison: Oral is safer, cheaper, and painless but slow and subject to first-
pass. IV has 100% bioavailability and quick onset for emergencies, but is invasive, 
requires asepsis, and cannot be reversed once injected. 
3. Sublingual: Quick onset, bypasses first-pass metabolism, and can be terminated 
by spitting. Examples: Nitroglycerin (angina), Buprenorphine (MI). 
4. Enemas: Evacuant enemas (e.g., soap water) are for bowel evacuation. Retention 
enemas (e.g., methylprednisolone for colitis) are for local or systemic absorption. 
5. IV Precautions: Inject slowly, ensure the needle is correctly in the vein, and 
maintain strict aseptic conditions to avoid phlebitis or tissue necrosis. 
6. Special Systems: Liposomes are phospholipid vesicles for targeted delivery. 
Monoclonal antibodies are cultured immunoglobulins that react with specific 
antigens. Both are used for targeted delivery, especially for anticancer drugs.
