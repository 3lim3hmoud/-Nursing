1. Bacterial Meningitis 
  Definition 
Inflammation of the arachnoid, pia mater, 
subarachnoid space, and CSF. A life-threatening 
disorder most often caused by bacteria or 
viruses. Bacteria enter the CNS via the 
bloodstream, penetrating trauma, invasive 
procedures, or ruptured abscess. 
  Causes 
• Bacteria — most serious (N. meningitidis, S. 
pneumoniae, H. influenzae) 
• Viruses, Fungi, Parasites (less common) 
• Risk factors: immunosuppression, extremes 
of age, crowded living, VP shunt, alcohol 
abuse, under-vaccination 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Severe headache, high fever & chills 
• Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck) 
• Nausea / vomiting, photophobia, irritability 
• Positive Kernig's sign & Brudzinski's sign 
• Signs of increased ICP, altered mental status 
• Purpuric rash (meningococcal), seizures 
  Complications 
• SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate ADH) 
• DIC — disseminated intravascular coagulation 
• Septic shock, Waterhouse-Friderichsen 
syndrome 
• Hydrocephalus, hearing loss 
• Permanent neurological deficits 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Risk for Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion 
r/t cerebral edema and increased ICP 
• Hyperthermia r/t infectious process 
• Acute Pain r/t meningeal irritation and 
photophobia 
• Risk for Injury r/t seizure activity and altered 
LOC 
• Disturbed Sensory Perception r/t 
neurological dysfunction 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Neurological assessment every 1–2 hours 
(LOC, pupils, motor) 
• Maintain quiet, dark environment — reduce 
stimulation 
• HOB elevated 30°, neutral head position 
• IV antibiotics STAT — empirical coverage 
while awaiting CSF results 
• Dexamethasone to reduce cerebral 
inflammation 
• Fever management: acetaminophen, cooling 
blanket 
• Droplet isolation precautions for first 24 hrs 
of antibiotics 
• Seizure precautions: padded rails, O₂ at 
bedside 
• Accurate I&O — monitor for SIADH (fluid 
restriction) vs DI 
• Monitor for Cushing's triad: widening pulse 
pressure, bradycardia, irregular respirations 
• ICP monitoring if indicated 
• Lumbar puncture findings: ↑ WBC, ↑ 
protein, ↓ glucose, cloudy CSF

2. Craniotomy 
  Indications 
• Tumor resection (primary or metastatic) 
• Cerebral decompression — relieve ICP 
• Evacuation of hematoma or abscess 
• Clipping or removal of aneurysm 
• AVM (arteriovenous malformation) 
treatment 
  Complications 
• Cerebral edema — peaks 48–72 hrs postop 
• Hemorrhage — watch for new neuro deficits 
• CSF leak — clear drainage from wound/nose 
(glucose ≥30 mg/dL) 
• Diabetes Insipidus — polyuria >200 mL/hr, SG 
≤1.005 
• Infection / meningitis — sterile technique 
essential 
• DVT — from prolonged immobility 
• SIADH — fluid & sodium imbalance 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Decreased Intracranial Adaptive Capacity r/t 
failure of normal intracranial compensatory 
mechanisms 
• Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion r/t 
cerebral edema and increased ICP 
• Acute Pain r/t surgical incision and ICP 
elevation 
• Risk for Infection r/t surgical wound and 
invasive monitoring devices 
• Disturbed Body Image r/t change in body 
structure and appearance 
  Patient Teaching 
• Avoid bending, lifting, straining, Valsalva 
maneuver 
• No coughing, sneezing, or nose-blowing 
postoperatively 
• Report clear drainage from wound — may 
indicate CSF leak 
• Report vision changes, seizures, weakness 
immediately 
• Activity restrictions — gradual return to 
normal 
• Wound care and dressing changes 
• Address fear of permanent neurological 
impairment 
  Nursing Interventions 
• HOB 30–45° at all times — facilitates venous 
drainage 
• Maintain neutral head position — avoid 
neck/hip flexion 
• Hourly neurological assessment (LOC, pupils, 
motor strength) 
• Hourly I&O — identify fluid imbalance early 
• Measure urine specific gravity if DI suspected 
• Pain: small IV morphine (ICU) → 
acetaminophen + codeine (oral) 
• Stool softeners — prevent straining which 
raises ICP & BP 
• Pulmonary care — space activities to prevent 
ICP spikes 
• Sterile technique for incision and drainage 
device care 
• Corneal protection: saline drops Q2h, cover 
eyes if comatose 
• Pad side rails — injury prevention during 
altered mentation 
• Fluid restriction if ordered (cerebral edema or 
SIADH) 
• Monitor for signs of meningitis — fever, 
nuchal rigidity

3. Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) 
  Definition 
Life-threatening hyperglycemic crisis in DM. 
Absent insulin → cells use fat for energy → 
ketone production → metabolic acidosis. The 
body converts glycogen (glycogenolysis) and 
protein to glucose, worsening hyperglycemia. 
  Causes 
• Infection — most common precipitating 
factor 
• Omission or inadequate insulin dose 
• New-onset undiagnosed Type 1 DM 
• Surgery, trauma, or major physical stress 
• Psychological stress + eating disorders in 
young DM patients 
• Increased metabolic demand (changes in 
insulin type/dosage) 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia, malaise, 
headache 
• Kussmaul respirations — deep and rapid (air 
hunger) 
• Fruity/acetone breath odor 
• Dehydration: dry skin, sunken eyes, 
hypotension, tachycardia 
• Nausea/vomiting, extreme fatigue, weight 
loss 
• CNS depression → confusion → coma if 
untreated 
  Investigations 
• Blood glucose > 250 mg/dL 
• Urine ketones — strongly positive 
• ABG: pH < 7.3, ↓ bicarbonate → metabolic 
acidosis 
• Low serum sodium (water shift from ICF to 
ECF) 
• Elevated serum potassium initially (acidosis 
drives K⁺ out of cells) 
• Elevated BUN / creatinine 
• CBC, electrolytes, renal/hepatic function 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Deficient Fluid Volume r/t osmotic diuresis 
and vomiting 
• Imbalanced Nutrition: Less than Body 
Requirements r/t insulin deficiency and 
cellular starvation 
• Decreased Cardiac Output r/t alterations in 
preload from fluid deficit 
• Risk for Electrolyte Imbalance r/t osmotic 
diuresis and insulin therapy 
• Anxiety r/t threat to biologic, psychologic, 
and social integrity 
  Nursing Management 
• IV fluids: 0.9% NaCl (isotonic) first → 0.45% 
NaCl if hypernatremia 
• Add dextrose when glucose reaches 200 
mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia 
• Regular insulin: IV bolus 0.1 unit/kg → 
infusion 0.1 unit/kg/hr 
• Target glucose drop: 50–70 mg/dL/hour 
• Monitor blood glucose EVERY HOUR 
• Potassium replacement: must be ≥3.3 mEq 
BEFORE starting insulin 
• Add 20–40 mEq/L K⁺ to IV fluids based on 
serum level 
• Bicarbonate: only if pH < 7.10 — give by slow 
IV infusion 
• NG tube if gastric distention, ileus, or 
vomiting 
• NPO during acute phase — advance diet as 
tolerated 
• Monitor for complications: fluid overload, 
hypoglycemia, cerebral edema 
• Transition to SC insulin when glucose 200 
mg/dL + acidosis corrected 
• Patient education: sick-day rules, insulin 
management, glucose monitoring

4. Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) 
  Definition 
Condition where blood clots form throughout 
the body, blocking small blood vessels. Micro-
clots impair tissue perfusion in major organs → 
hypoxia, ischemia, tissue damage. Clotting 
factors are consumed → simultaneous 
bleeding. Always secondary to an underlying 
disorder. 
  Causes 
• Infection — bacterial (Gram+/−), viral, fungal, 
protozoan 
• Cancer — especially acute promyelocytic 
leukemia (APL) 
• Obstetric — abruptio placentae, eclampsia, 
postpartum hemorrhage 
• Massive tissue injury — trauma, burns, 
hyperthermia, surgery 
• Transfusion reaction — ABO blood group 
incompatibility 
• Septic shock (most common ICU trigger) 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Bleeding from multiple body sites 
simultaneously 
• Purpura, petechiae, hematomas, ecchymosis 
• Prolonged oozing from IV sites — FIRST sign 
• Hypotension, tachycardia, signs of shock 
• Hematuria, hemoptysis, GI bleeding 
• Blood clots: DVT, pulmonary embolism 
• Confusion, fever, oliguria, cyanosis 
  Investigations 
• PT > 15 seconds (prolonged) 
• PTT > 60–90 seconds (prolonged) 
• Fibrinogen — low (consumed) 
• D-dimer / FSPs — HIGH (fibrin split products) 
• Platelet count — falling / thrombocytopenia 
• ISTH score ≥ 5 = DIC confirmed 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Ineffective Tissue Perfusion r/t microthrombi 
formation and hemorrhage 
• Risk for Bleeding r/t depletion of clotting 
factors and platelets 
• Impaired Gas Exchange r/t hemorrhage and 
microemboli in pulmonary vasculature 
• Acute Pain r/t tissue ischemia from 
microthrombi 
• Fear r/t life-threatening condition and 
intensive interventions 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Treat underlying cause FIRST (antibiotics for 
infection, evacuate uterus in obstetric DIC) 
• Assess LOC every 1–2 hrs — 
restlessness/irritability = early cerebral 
hypoxia 
• Monitor O₂ saturation — keep ≥ 90%; 
monitor ABGs 
• Position: high-Fowler's — promotes lung 
expansion 
• Reposition every 2 hrs + chest physiotherapy 
• Monitor HR and BP — tachycardia + 
hypotension = ↓ cardiac output 
• Inspect skin for petechiae, purpura, oozing 
from IV sites 
• Administer blood products: RBCs, FFP, 
platelets, cryoprecipitate 
• Monitor coagulation labs: PT, PTT, fibrinogen, 
D-dimer 
  Bleeding Precautions 
• Avoid IM injections and unnecessary 
venipunctures 
• Use oral or SC medication routes instead 
• Gentle oral care — saline rinses instead of 
toothbrushes 
• Apply firm pressure ≥ 5 min to all 
venipuncture sites 
• Prevent falls — padded side rails, non-slip 
footwear 
• Avoid medications that impair platelet 
function (NSAIDs, aspirin)

5. Hepatic Encephalopathy 
  Definition 
Life-threatening complication of liver failure. 
The neuropsychiatric manifestation of hepatic 
failure associated with portal hypertension and 
shunting of portal blood (containing ammonia) 
directly into systemic circulation — bypassing 
liver detoxification. 
  Causes 
• Hepatic insufficiency — liver cannot detoxify 
metabolic byproducts 
• Portal-systemic shunting — collateral vessels 
bypass liver 
• GI bleeding — esophageal varices, chronic GI 
bleed 
• High-protein diet → excess ammonia 
production 
• Bacterial infection / uremia 
• Alkalosis or hypokalemia — increases 
ammonia absorption 
• Medications: sedatives, tranquilizers, 
analgesics, diuretics 
• Excessive diuresis, dehydration, surgery, 
fever 
  Stages 
• Stage 1: Slight confusion, mood/sleep 
changes, sleep during day (insomnia at night) 
• Stage 2: Drowsy, asterixis (hand flapping), 
disorientation to time 
• Stage 3: Difficult to awaken, complete 
disorientation, marked confusion 
• Stage 4: Coma, possible seizures, no response 
to stimuli 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Mental status changes — from mild 
confusion to coma 
• Sleep-wake cycle reversal 
• Asterixis — flapping tremor of outstretched 
hands 
• Progressive disorientation, seizures 
• EEG: generalized slowing, triphasic waves 
• ↑ Serum ammonia level 
• Deteriorating handwriting and arithmetic 
scores 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Lactulose: decreases colonic pH → reduces 
ammonia absorption 
• Neomycin / Rifaximin: destroy bowel bacteria 
→ reduce ammonia production 
• Diet: protein 1.0–1.5 g/kg/day (do NOT over-
restrict) 
• Neurological assessment frequently — record 
handwriting & arithmetic daily 
• Monitor mental status (LOC, behavior, 
orientation) 
• Accurate daily I&O, body weight, vital signs 
Q4h 
• Safe environment: prevent injury, bleeding, 
infection 
• Encourage deep breathing + position changes 
— prevent pneumonia 
• Assess for potential infection sites 
(peritoneum, lungs) — report promptly 
  Patient Teaching 
• Family: watch for subtle early signs of 
recurrence after discharge — mild confusion, 
personality changes, sleep disturbance 
• Diet: moderate-protein, high-calorie (protein 
1.0–1.5 g/kg) 
• In acute phase: reduce protein to 0.8 g/kg 
briefly 
• Add protein back gradually: 10 g increments 
every 3–5 days if mental status stable 
• Never abruptly restrict protein long-term — 
causes muscle wasting 
• Avoid alcohol and all hepatotoxic 
drugs/supplements 
• Take lactulose as prescribed (target: 2–4 soft 
stools/day) 
• Regular follow-up appointments and lab 
monitoring 
• Report confusion, personality changes, 
increased drowsiness immediately

6. Hypertensive Crisis 
  Definition 
Severe elevation of BP > 180/110 mmHg that 
can lead to stroke and end-organ damage. 
Classified into two types based on presence or 
absence of acute target organ damage. 
  Types: Urgency vs Emergency 
• URGENCY: Severe BP elevation — NO acute 
organ damage. Treated with oral 
antihypertensives. Can be managed in 
outpatient or ED setting. 
• EMERGENCY: Severe BP elevation + acute 
end-organ damage (brain, heart, kidneys). 
Requires IV treatment in ICU. Life-
threatening. 
  Causes 
• Primary (essential) hypertension — most 
common 
• Abrupt withdrawal of antihypertensive drugs 
• Pheochromocytoma (catecholamine surge) 
• Eclampsia / pregnancy-induced hypertension 
• Renal artery stenosis / renovascular disease 
• Myocardial ischemia, aortic dissection 
• Stimulant drugs (cocaine, amphetamines) 
  Clinical Manifestations 
Emergency: 
• Severe headache, altered LOC, papilledema 
• Chest pain / angina, pulmonary edema, 
dyspnea 
• Oliguria, hematuria — renal involvement 
• Neurological deficits, visual changes 
Urgency: 
• Severe headache, epistaxis (nosebleeds) 
• Severe anxiety, shortness of breath — NO 
organ damage 
  Complications 
• Stroke, hypertensive encephalopathy 
• Myocardial infarction (MI), aortic dissection 
• Acute renal failure, pulmonary edema 
• Retinal damage, blindness 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Decreased Cardiac Output r/t increased 
systemic vascular resistance 
• Risk for Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion 
r/t hypertensive encephalopathy and CVA 
• Excess Fluid Volume r/t sodium and water 
retention 
• Anxiety r/t change in health status and threat 
to biologic integrity 
• Deficient Knowledge r/t disease process and 
antihypertensive regimen 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Continuous hemodynamic monitoring — 
arterial line preferred 
• IV antihypertensives: nitroprusside, labetalol, 
nicardipine 
• BP reduction target: ↓25% in first hour → 
160/100 over next 6 hrs → gradual over days 
• Exception — aortic dissection: target systolic 
< 100 mmHg if tolerated 
• Neurological assessment Q1h — monitor for 
stroke signs 
• Urinary catheter — monitor output Q1h 
(renal perfusion) 
• Continuous cardiac monitoring (ECG) 
• Seizure precautions, bed rest in quiet 
environment 
• CBC, electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, urinalysis, 
chest X-ray, ECG 
  Patient Education 
• Take antihypertensive medications DAILY — 
never stop abruptly 
• Low-sodium diet (avoid canned foods, 
processed meats, soy sauce) 
• Weight loss + regular physical activity 30 
min/day 
• Limit alcohol, stop smoking 
• Regular home BP monitoring — know target 
values 
• Stress management: yoga, relaxation 
techniques 
• Report severe headache, visual changes, or 
chest pain immediately

• Follow-up: Q1–2 weeks until target achieved, 
then Q6 months

7. Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP) 
  Definition 
Sustained increase in pressure within the rigid 
cranial vault > 20 mmHg. The vault contains 3 
non-compressible components: brain tissue, 
blood, and CSF. If one increases, others must 
decrease (Monro-Kellie doctrine) or ICP rises. 
Normal ICP: 0–15 mmHg. CPP = MAP − ICP. 
Goal: CPP 60–70 mmHg. 
  Causes 
• Traumatic brain injury (TBI) 
• Stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic) 
• Brain tumor or abscess 
• Hydrocephalus 
• Cerebral edema (post-surgery, infection) 
• Subarachnoid or intracerebral hemorrhage 
• Meningitis / encephalitis 
  Clinical Manifestations — Cushing's 
Triad 
• Widening pulse pressure (systolic ↑, diastolic 
↓) 
• Bradycardia 
• Irregular respirations (Cheyne-Stokes) 
Other signs: 
• ↓ LOC — EARLIEST and most sensitive sign 
• Pupillary changes: unequal, sluggish, or fixed 
& dilated 
• Vomiting without nausea (projectile) 
• Papilledema (blurred disc margins on 
fundoscopy) 
• Morning headache (worse lying down) 
• Decorticate → decerebrate posturing (late, 
severe) 
  Investigations 
• CT scan — first-line (identify blood, edema, 
tumor) 
• MRI — more detailed structural imaging 
• ICP monitoring: EVD (gold standard), 
fiberoptic probe 
• ABGs — monitor for hypoxia (PaO₂) and CO₂ 
(ventilation) 
• Lumbar puncture — CONTRAINDICATED if ICP 
is elevated 
  Nursing Precautions 
• HOB at 30° — neutral head position at all 
times 
• Avoid neck/hip flexion — impairs venous 
drainage 
• Space ALL nursing activities — allow rest 
between turns/hygiene 
• Avoid Valsalva maneuver: no straining, give 
stool softeners 
• Minimize sensory stimulation — noise, 
painful stimuli, bright light 
• Seizure precautions — padded rails, suction 
at bedside 
• Accurate hourly I&O — monitor for DI 
(polyuria) vs SIADH 
• Assess physiologic response to visitors — 
limit if ICP rises 
  Medical Management 
• Osmotic diuretics: 25% Mannitol or 3% 
Hypertonic Saline 
• Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) — for 
tumor/abscess-related edema 
• Controlled hyperventilation (intubated): PCO₂ 
target 30–35 mmHg 
• CSF drainage via EVD — reduces ICP rapidly 
• Sedation and paralysis — reduce metabolic 
demands 
• Temperature management — prevent 
hyperthermia (worsens edema) 
• Decompressive craniectomy — last resort for 
refractory ICP

• Antipyretics, maintain normoglycemia, 
correct hyponatremia

8. Liver Dialysis 
  Definition 
A system designed to remove toxins not cleared 
by the failing liver. Targets lipophilic and 
albumin-bound substances (bilirubin, bile 
acids), metabolites of aromatic amino acids, 
and medium-sized molecules that accumulate 
in liver failure and cause organ dysfunction. 
  Types of Systems 
• BIOLOGICAL systems: contain functioning 
liver cells → provide BOTH detoxifying AND 
synthetic liver functions. More complex and 
expensive. 
• NON-BIOLOGICAL systems: no living cells → 
provide detoxification ONLY (no synthetic 
function). Examples: MARS (Molecular 
Adsorbent Recirculating System), 
Prometheus. Simpler and cheaper. 
  Settings Used In 
• Transplantation centers 
• Intensive Care Units (ICU) 
• Specialized dialysis units 
  Indications 
• Acute hepatic encephalopathy from 
worsening chronic liver disease (acute-on-
chronic) or fulminant hepatic failure 
• Severe drug overdose — acetaminophen 
toxicity, tricyclic antidepressants 
• Acute decompensation of chronic liver 
disease 
• Acute liver failure awaiting transplantation 
(bridge to transplant) 
• Liver dysfunction following transplantation 
(primary graft non-function) 
  Complications 
• Thrombocytopenia — platelet destruction 
during circuit 
• Coagulopathy — clotting factor loss 
• Hypotension — hemodynamic instability 
during sessions 
• Electrolyte imbalances — monitor Na⁺, K⁺, 
Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ 
• Infection risk — from extracorporeal circuit 
• Long-term side effects — patients must be 
counseled before treatment 
  Clinical Efficiency / Benefits 
• Complete recovery of liver function following 
recompensation (acute-on-chronic failure) 
• Bridge short-term or long-term waiting times 
until liver transplantation 
• Increasing the survival rate of liver 
transplants — better patient condition at time 
of transplant 
• Improving quality of life during liver failure — 
reduces encephalopathy, pruritus, fatigue 
• Reduction in bilirubin, bile acids, ammonia, 
and other toxin levels 
• May improve hemodynamic stability in 
hepatorenal syndrome 
  Key Nursing Points 
• Monitor vital signs and hemodynamics 
throughout session 
• Assess neurological status — improvement in 
encephalopathy 
• Monitor coagulation labs: PT, PTT, platelet 
count before and after 
• Ensure IV access is adequate — large-bore or 
central line 
• Document fluid balance accurately during 
treatment 
• Patient/family education: purpose, 
procedure, expected outcomes

9. Liver Transplantation 
  Definition 
Partial or total removal of the diseased liver and 
its replacement with a healthy donor liver in the 
same anatomic location (orthotopic). Used to 
treat life-threatening, end-stage liver disease 
for which no other treatment is available. 
  Indications 
• End-stage liver disease (cirrhosis) from any 
cause 
• Fulminant hepatic failure — acute liver failure 
• Hepatocellular carcinoma (within Milan 
criteria) 
• Biliary atresia (common indication in 
children) 
• Metabolic liver diseases (Wilson's disease, α₁-
antitrypsin deficiency) 
• Primary sclerosing cholangitis, primary biliary 
cirrhosis 
  Contraindications 
• Sepsis — active systemic infection 
• Malignancies outside the liver 
• Active substance abuse (alcohol, drugs) 
• Irreversible brain damage 
• Inability to comply with post-transplant 
immunosuppression 
• AIDS (active), active cardiopulmonary disease 
  Types of Rejection 
• HYPERACUTE: Within minutes to hours 
(extremely rare in liver). Antibody-mediated. 
Usually leads to immediate graft loss. 
• ACUTE (most common): Days to weeks post-
transplant. Cell-mediated. Treated with high-
dose IV corticosteroids. Most cases respond 
well. 
• CHRONIC: Months to years post-transplant. 
Progressive bile duct damage. Leads to graft 
failure. May require re-transplantation. 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Risk for Infection r/t immunosuppressive 
therapy and surgical wound 
• Risk for Bleeding r/t coagulopathy and 
surgical trauma 
• Impaired Fluid Volume r/t third spacing, 
hemorrhage, and low oncotic pressure 
• Anxiety r/t uncertainty of graft function and 
complex postoperative care 
• Deficient Knowledge r/t immunosuppression 
regimen and rejection signs 
  Nursing Care 
• Hemodynamic monitoring: watch for 
hypotension during warming phase, 
hypertension from pain/stress 
• Fluid resuscitation: crystalloids (NS 0.9%), 
albumin, blood products as ordered 
• Monitor renal function closely — nephrotoxic 
immunosuppressants: cyclosporine, 
tacrolimus 
• Watch for infection signs — 
immunosuppression increases risk 
significantly 
• Wound care — sterile technique; inspect 
incision for dehiscence or infection 
• Accurate I&O — monitor for oliguria 
(hepatorenal syndrome may persist) 
• Administer immunosuppressants on time — 
tacrolimus, mycophenolate, steroids 
• Assess for rejection: fever, jaundice, right 
upper quadrant pain, elevated LFTs 
• Manage ascites: sodium restriction, diuretics 
(spironolactone + furosemide) 
• Monitor coagulation: PT, PTT — correct with 
FFP, Vitamin K 
• Patient education: rejection signs, infection 
prevention, medication compliance

10. Nutritional Support in Critical Care 
  Enteral Nutrition (EN) — Definition 
Nutrition delivered via the GI tract (tube 
feeding). Routes: nasogastric (NGT), 
nasoduodenal, jejunostomy (PEG/PEJ). 
Preferred over TPN when GI tract is functional. 
  EN — Indications 
• Functioning GI tract with inability to eat orally 
• Burns, trauma, head injury, sepsis 
• Post-surgery when GI function returns 
• Neurological impairment affecting swallowing 
  EN — Complications 
• Aspiration pneumonia — most dangerous 
(keep HOB 30–45°) 
• Diarrhea — most common complication in 
ICU 
• Tube displacement or blockage 
• Abdominal distension, constipation 
• Refeeding syndrome — hypophosphatemia, 
hypokalemia 
• Sinusitis (nasogastric route) 
  TPN — Definition 
Delivery of ALL nutrients by IV infusion, 
completely bypassing the GI tract. 
Recommended when oral or tube feeding is 
contraindicated or inadequate for more than 5–
7 days. 
  TPN — Indications 
• Non-functioning GI tract / prolonged ileus 
• Short bowel syndrome, severe malabsorption 
• GI fistula, bowel obstruction 
• Severe acute pancreatitis (initially) 
• Severe inflammatory bowel disease 
  TPN — Complications 
• Sepsis / infection — most serious (strict 
asepsis required) 
• Hyperglycemia — most common metabolic 
complication 
• Hypoglycemia if stopped abruptly 
• Electrolyte imbalances — Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, 
Phosphate 
• Refeeding syndrome 
• Liver dysfunction (cholestasis) with long-term 
use 
• Pneumothorax — from central line insertion 
• Thrombosis at catheter site 
  Nursing Care 
• Accurately record all food and fluids 
consumed (type + amount) 
• Calculate daily protein and calorie intake 
• Anthropometric measurements: height, 
weight, BMI, triceps skinfold, mid-arm 
circumference 
Enteral Feeding Nursing Care: 
• Verify tube placement before each feeding 
(X-ray for initial; pH / aspiration for ongoing) 
• Keep HOB elevated 30–45° continuously 
during feeding 
• Check gastric residual volume every 4–6 hrs 
— hold if >250 mL 
• Monitor bowel sounds and abdominal 
distension 
TPN Nursing Care: 
• Use volumetric infusion pump — precise rate 
control 
• Change TPN bag and tubing every 24 hours 
(strict aseptic technique) 
• Monitor blood glucose every 4–6 hrs — treat 
hyperglycemia with insulin 
• Monitor electrolytes, albumin, pre-albumin 
daily initially 
• Inspect central line insertion site for signs of 
infection 
• NEVER stop TPN abruptly — taper slowly to 
prevent rebound hypoglycemia 
• Nursing diagnoses: Imbalanced Nutrition r/t 
malabsorption; Risk for Infection r/t IV access

11. Renal Transplantation 
  Contraindications 
ABSOLUTE: 
• Age < 18 years (living donor) 
• Uncontrolled hypertension 
• Diabetes mellitus (for living donor) 
• Proteinuria > 300 mg / 24 hours 
• Abnormal GFR for age 
• Microscopic hematuria 
• High risk of thromboembolism 
• Significant chronic illness: lung disease, 
recent malignancy, heart disease 
• History of bilateral kidney stones 
• HIV positive 
RELATIVE: 
• Anatomical anomalies of the graft 
• Active chronic infection (TB, Hepatitis B/C, 
parasites) 
• Obesity, psychiatric disorders 
  Nursing Care 
Immediate Postoperative: 
• Maintain fluid & electrolyte balance — strict 
I&O 
• Wound care — sterile dressing changes 
• Pain management around the clock 
• Pulmonary hygiene: incentive spirometry + 
early ambulation 
• Restore normal bowel function (ileus risk 
from steroids) 
Long-term: 
• Lifelong follow-up by transplant specialist 
Q6–12 months 
• Renal function + immunosuppression 
monitoring Q4–8 weeks 
• Annual dermatological exam, tumor 
screening, chest X-ray 
• Monitor proteinuria, BP, lipids, glucose, bone 
density 
  Complications 
• Infection — leading cause of morbidity 
(immunosuppressed) 
• CHF — most common cause of hospitalization 
• Rejection (hyperacute, acute, chronic) 
• Hypertension, renal failure 
• Metabolic complications: hyperglycemia, 
dyslipidemia 
  Patient Teaching 
Call your doctor immediately if: 
• Fever > 38°C (100°F) 
• Flu-like symptoms: chills, aches, dizziness, 
nausea/vomiting 
• New pain or tenderness around transplanted 
kidney 
• Fluid retention / swelling 
• Weight gain > 1–2 kg within 24 hours 
• Significant decrease in urine output 
Lifestyle: 
• Exercise: 20–30 min, 3–4 times/week 
• Wait 1.5 hrs after eating before exercising 
• Avoid heavy lifting, pushing, or strenuous 
exercise initially 
• Wear protective footwear, dress 
appropriately 
Medication: 
• Take immunosuppressants EXACTLY as 
prescribed — NEVER skip doses 
• Avoid NSAIDs and nephrotoxic agents 
• Take beta-blockers as prescribed — do NOT 
stop abruptly 
• Sun protection — increased skin cancer risk 
• Follow recommended diet: low sodium, low 
fat if needed

12. Seizures 
  Definition 
Repetitive depolarization of hyperactive and 
hypersensitive brain cells → abnormal electrical 
activity throughout the brain. Uncontrolled 
discharge propagates through neural pathways, 
producing clinical manifestations (motor, 
sensory, autonomic, behavioral). 
  Causes 
• Epilepsy — most common chronic cause 
• Drug or alcohol abuse / withdrawal 
• Drug toxicity — aminophylline, lidocaine, 
isoniazid 
• Recent head injury / TBI 
• CNS infection — meningitis, encephalitis 
• CNS tumor, abscess 
• Acute metabolic disturbances: hypoglycemia, 
hyponatremia, hypocalcemia, renal failure 
• Cerebrovascular accident (CVA) 
• Hypoxemia, high fever 
  Nursing Care DURING Seizure 
• PRIMARY goal: Protect airway and prevent 
injury 
• Position: side-lying — facilitates airway 
clearance 
• Do NOT restrain the patient — guide 
movements gently 
• Do NOT force an airway into the mouth — 
risk of dental/jaw injury 
• Clear area of hard/sharp objects 
• Observe and document: seizure type, point of 
origin, spread, duration 
• Note: automatisms (lip smacking, repeated 
swallowing), bowel/bladder incontinence, 
tongue biting 
• Assess LOC before, during, and after 
• Administer O₂ via mask + suction as needed 
Medications: 
• Lorazepam (Ativan): 0.1 mg/kg IV — up to 4 
mg; max 2 mg/min; may repeat in 5–10 min 
• Midazolam: 0.2 mg/kg IM — up to 10 mg max 
• Diazepam (Valium): 0.15 mg/kg IV — max 10 
mg; max 5 mg/min 
• Phenytoin (Dilantin): 20 mg/kg IV — max 50 
mg/min (long-acting) 
  Nursing Care POSTICTAL 
• Assess vital signs closely — provide ECG 
monitoring 
• Monitor oxygenation: ABGs, SpO₂, breath 
sounds 
• Maintain side-lying position — aspiration risk 
• Check orientation, ability to speak (patient 
usually sleeps afterward) 
• Note: headache, myalgia, fatigue, confusion 
(normal postictal features) 
• Document seizure characteristics completely: 
onset, duration, type, spread, recovery time 
• Obtain: EEG, CT/MRI, serum drug screen, 
electrolytes (BUN, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, glucose), CBC 
• Provide emotional support — seizures are 
frightening and disorienting 
• Reorient patient calmly as consciousness 
returns 
• Continue seizure precautions: padded rails, 
O₂ at bedside, suction available 
  Key Assessment Questions 
• Identify precipitating factors and preceding 
aura 
• What was patient doing at the time of 
seizure? 
• Were there unusual symptoms before 
(movements, sensations, smells)? 
• Did patient know a seizure was coming? 
• History of prior seizures, medications, 
compliance?

13. Stroke ⭐⭐⭐ 
  Definition & Types 
Sudden onset of acute neurological deficit 
persisting > 24 hours, caused by interruption of 
blood flow to the brain. TIA: same but resolves 
in < 24 hrs (usually < 1 hr). 
• ISCHEMIC — 87%: Thrombotic (75%) | 
Embolic (25%) 
• HEMORRHAGIC — 13%: Intracerebral (10%) | 
Subarachnoid (3%) 
  Causes 
• Ischemic: vascular obstruction (thrombus or 
embolus), small penetrating artery 
thrombosis, cardiac embolism (atrial 
fibrillation) 
• Hemorrhagic: hypertensive vascular disease, 
ruptured aneurysm (weakness in artery wall), 
ruptured AVM (common in young people) 
  Risk Factors 
• Hypertension — most important modifiable 
risk factor 
• Atrial fibrillation, DM, hyperlipidemia, 
obesity, smoking 
• Age, family history, prior TIA or stroke 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Numbness/weakness of face, arm, or leg (one 
side) 
• Confusion, trouble speaking (dysarthria) or 
understanding (aphasia) 
• Visual: diplopia, homonymous hemianopsia, 
loss of peripheral vision 
• Hemiparesis/hemiplegia, ataxia, dysphagia 
  Investigations 
• CT without contrast — FIRST-LINE: 
differentiates hemorrhagic from ischemic 
• MRI — detects infarction earlier; determines 
type, size, location 
• CT/MRI Angiography — confirms aneurysm or 
AVM 
• 12-lead ECG + continuous cardiac monitoring 
• Echocardiography (for cardioembolic source) 
• CBC, electrolytes, glucose, renal/hepatic 
function, coagulation 
• ABGs if hypoxia suspected, Transcranial 
Doppler 
  rtPA (Thrombolytic Therapy) 
For ISCHEMIC stroke ONLY: 
• Dose: 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) 
• 10% as IV bolus over 1–2 min; 90% infusion 
over 60 min 
• Window: ≤ 4.5 hours from symptom onset 
• No antithrombotic therapy for 24 hours after 
rtPA 
• Risk: intracerebral hemorrhage ~6.4% 
• Inclusion: ischemic diagnosis confirmed, SBP 
≤185 mmHg, DBP ≤110 mmHg, not minor or 
rapidly resolving stroke 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion r/t 
interruption of cerebral blood flow 
• Impaired Physical Mobility r/t hemiplegia and 
neuromuscular impairment 
• Impaired Verbal Communication r/t aphasia 
from cerebral damage 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Neurological assessment Q1–2h: LOC, pupils, 
motor strength, speech, facial droop 
• Airway management — priority; O₂ therapy 
for SpO₂ > 94% 
• HOB at 30° — reduces ICP and aspiration risk 
• Monitor BP — for ischemic: allow permissive 
hypertension (do NOT aggressively lower 
unless >220/120 or before rtPA); for 
hemorrhagic: target SBP <140 mmHg 
• Dysphagia screening before ANY oral intake 
— bedside swallowing test 
• NPO if dysphagia confirmed — consult 
speech therapy 
• Aspiration precautions: HOB up, thickened 
liquids if ordered 
• DVT prophylaxis: compression stockings, 
sequential compression devices 
• Bladder care: intermittent catheterization 
preferred over indwelling 
• Skin integrity: reposition Q2h, pressure injury 
prevention 
• Emotional support: explain aphasia/paralysis 
to patient AND family 
• Early rehabilitation: physiotherapy, OT, 
speech therapy from day 1 
• Anticoagulation for embolic stroke (heparin 
→ warfarin) 
• Antiplatelet therapy: aspirin or clopidogrel for 
thrombotic stroke 
• Monitor glucose — maintain normoglycemia 
(hyperglycemia worsens outcomes)

• Hemorrhagic: 'exploding headache,' 
decreased LOC, nuchal rigidity 
• Emotional lability, loss of self-control 
• Risk for Aspiration r/t dysphagia and reduced 
LOC

14. Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis) 
  Definition 
Severe, life-threatening form of 
hyperthyroidism with abrupt sudden onset. 
Represents extreme hypermetabolic state from 
excessive thyroid hormone. Almost always fatal 
if untreated. Requires ICU-level care. 
  Causes (Precipitating Triggers) 
In a patient with pre-existing hyperthyroidism: 
• Infection — most common trigger 
• Surgery or trauma — any major surgical 
procedure 
• Pregnancy or delivery 
• Abrupt withdrawal of antithyroid medications 
• Amiodarone — causes thyroid dysfunction in 
10–20% of patients 
• Major physical or psychological stress 
  Clinical Manifestations 
Cardiac: 
• Tachycardia, atrial fibrillation/PVCs, 
palpitations 
• Hypertension, chest pain, acute heart failure, 
pulmonary edema 
Neurological: 
• Hyperkinesis, nervousness, muscle weakness 
• Confusion, convulsions, fine tremor, 
psychosis, diaphoresis 
GI: 
• N/V, diarrhea, hepatomegaly, abdominal 
pain, weight loss 
Thermoregulatory: 
• High fever (hyperthermia), profuse sweating 
  Investigations 
• Free T3 and T4 — significantly elevated 
• TSH — suppressed or undetectable 
• CBC, liver enzymes, glucose, electrolytes 
• Cortisol level (stress response) 
• ECG — tachyarrhythmias, atrial fibrillation 
  Nursing Interventions 
Medication Sequence (critical order): 
• 1. Beta-blockers (propranolol): FIRST — 
blocks cardiovascular effects of excess 
hormone 
• 2. PTU (propylthiouracil): SECOND — blocks 
NEW hormone synthesis 
• 3. Iodine (Lugol's): 1–2 hrs AFTER PTU — 
blocks hormone RELEASE (if given before PTU, 
used to make more hormone) 
• Acetaminophen for fever — NOT aspirin 
(aspirin displaces T3/T4 from protein binding 
→ worsens crisis) 
• Cooling blanket, ice packs to axilla/groin, 
tepid sponge bath 
• IV fluids: large volumes glucose + isotonic 
sodium to replace losses 
• Insulin for hyperglycemia; antibiotics if 
infection present 
• Calm environment for agitated patient — 
reassure patient/family 
• Monitor heart rate closely — should decrease 
with beta-blockade 
• Frequent temperature monitoring until 
normal 
• Monitor for hyponatremia 
  Patient Teaching 
• Take antithyroid medications EXACTLY as 
prescribed — NEVER stop suddenly 
• Avoid iodine-rich foods: seafood, iodized salt, 
kelp 
• Avoid stimulants: caffeine, decongestants, 
OTC cold medications 
• Report immediately: palpitations, fever, 
tremor, worsening symptoms 
• Regular thyroid function tests (T3, T4, TSH) as 
scheduled 
• Stress management techniques — stress 
triggers thyroid storm 
• For patients on antithyroid drugs: report 
signs of agranulocytosis — sudden sore 
throat, fever, cough (stop medication, call 
doctor) 
• For beta-blocker patients: report bradycardia, 
unexplained fatigue, orthostatic hypotension 
• Wear medical alert bracelet identifying 
thyroid condition 
• Regular follow-up appointments — do not 
miss 
  Nursing Diagnoses 
• Decreased Cardiac Output r/t 
tachyarrhythmias and increased metabolic 
demands 
• Hyperthermia r/t hypermetabolic state and 
excessive heat production 
• Deficient Fluid Volume r/t profuse 
diaphoresis, vomiting, and diarrhea

• Anxiety / Acute Confusion r/t excess thyroid 
hormone affecting CNS 
• Deficient Knowledge r/t disease process and 
long-term medication management

15. Myasthenia Gravis (MG) 
  Definition 
Chronic autoimmune neuromuscular disorder 
producing sporadic but progressive weakness 
and abnormal fatigue in skeletal muscles. 
Antibodies block, destroy, or weaken ACh 
receptor sites at the neuromuscular junction → 
muscles cannot contract properly. 
Weakness WORSENS with exercise/repeated 
movement, IMPROVES with rest. 
  Clinical Manifestations 
• Gradual progressive skeletal muscle 
weakness worsening throughout the day 
• Ptosis — drooping of one or both eyelids 
(hallmark early sign) 
• Diplopia — double vision (extraocular muscle 
weakness) 
• Blank/mask-like facial expression (facial 
muscle weakness) 
• Dysphagia — difficulty swallowing 
• Dysarthria — slurred speech, difficulty 
chewing 
• Difficulty with lateral head motion 
• Respiratory failure if respiratory muscles 
involved — MEDICAL EMERGENCY 
  Investigations 
• Tensilon Test (neostigmine IV): temporary 
improvement = positive = MG confirmed. 
Long-standing ocular dysfunction may not 
respond. 
• EMG — electromyography: differentiates 
nerve disorders from muscle disorders 
• Nerve conduction studies: test for ACh 
receptor antibodies 
• CT chest: detect thymoma (associated in 
many MG patients) 
• Serum anti-AChR antibodies — positive in 
85% of MG 
  Myasthenic Crisis 
Emergency: sudden severe respiratory distress 
due to undermedication or triggers (infection, 
stress, surgery, extreme temperature). 
• Signs: acute respiratory distress, increased 
muscle weakness, difficulty talking/chewing, 
tachycardia, tachypnea 
• Tx: Tracheotomy, positive-pressure 
ventilation, vigorous suctioning 
• IV neostigmine or pyridostigmine; 
plasmapheresis to remove antibodies; IVIG 
• ⚠️ Anticholinesterases NOT effective during 
crisis — HOLD until respiratory function 
improves 
  Nursing Interventions 
• Establish neurologic and respiratory BASELINE 
— before each shift 
• Help remove secretions as they accumulate 
— suction if needed 
• Be ALERT for signs of impending crisis: 
increasing weakness, respiratory distress, 
difficulty talking 
• Administer anticholinesterase drugs at 
EVENLY SPACED INTERVALS on time as 
ordered — timing is critical 
• Be prepared to give ATROPINE for 
anticholinesterase overdose or toxicity 
• Plan exercise, meals, and activities at PEAKS 
in patient's energy level 
• Provide soft, solid foods (NOT liquids) — 
reduces choking risk 
• Always sit patient upright to eat 
• Respiratory monitoring: SpO₂, breath sounds, 
respiratory rate 
• Encourage patient to take active role in care 
decisions 
  Patient Teaching 
• Recognize adverse effects of 
anticholinesterase drugs: headaches, 
weakness, sweating, abdominal cramps, 
N/V/D, excessive salivation, bronchospasm — 
cholinergic crisis (opposite of MG: too much 
medication) 
• Take medications on time EVERY DAY — 
timing is critical

• Plan daily activities at energy peaks; frequent 
rest periods throughout the day 
• Avoid: strenuous exercise, stress, infection, 
extreme temperature, sun exposure 
• Avoid new medications without consulting 
physician first 
• Carry medical alert ID at all times 
• Report worsening symptoms immediately — 
do not wait
