• Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is 
defined as a persistent elevation of arterial 
blood pressure.
According to the World Health Organization 
(WHO) and American Heart Association 
(AHA), it is diagnosed when:
• Systolic blood pressure (SBP) is 140 mmHg 
or higher, and/or
• Diastolic blood pressure (DBP) is 90 mmHg 
or higher,
measured on two or more separate 
occasions.

Hypertension

• Blood pressure is determined by two main factors:
• Cardiac output – the amount of blood the heart pumps.
• Peripheral vascular resistance – the resistance of 
blood vessels to blood flow.
• Hypertension occurs when there is increased cardiac 
output, increased vascular resistance, or both.
Contributing mechanisms include:
• Overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system
• Overstimulation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone 
system (RAAS)
• Endothelial dysfunction
• Sodium and water retention

Primary (Essential) Hypertension
–Accounts for 90–95% of 
cases.
–No single identifiable cause.
–Risk factors include genetics, 
age, obesity, stress, 
sedentary lifestyle, and high 
salt intake.

Secondary Hypertension
Has a specific underlying cause, such 
as:
•Chronic kidney disease
•Endocrine disorders (e.g., 
Cushing’s syndrome, 
hyperthyroidism, 
pheochromocytoma)
•Certain medications (like oral 
contraceptives or steroids)

Risk Factors
•Non-modifiable: Age, family 
history, ethnicity, and gender
•Modifiable: Obesity, high salt 
intake, smoking, alcohol use, 
physical inactivity, stress, and 
unhealthy diet

Clinical Manifestations
• Hypertension is often called the “silent killer” because many 
patients are asymptomatic.
However, symptoms can appear when blood pressure is 
extremely high, including:
• Headache (especially in the morning)
• Dizziness
• Blurred vision
• Chest pain
• Shortness of breath
• Nosebleeds (epistaxis)

Complications
• Untreated or poorly controlled hypertension can 
lead to target organ damage, affecting:
• Heart: Left ventricular hypertrophy, heart failure, 
myocardial infarction
• Brain: Stroke, transient ischemic attack
• Kidneys: Chronic kidney disease
• Eyes: Retinopathy
• Blood vessels: Peripheral arterial disease

Diagnosis
• Accurate blood pressure measurement using 
proper technique.
• Multiple readings on different days.
• Laboratory tests to assess organ function and 
identify causes:
–Blood tests: Creatinine, electrolytes, glucose, 
lipids
–Urinalysis for protein or albumin
• ECG and sometimes echocardiogram

Management
Lifestyle Modifications (First-line for all patients):
• Reduce salt intake (<5 grams per day)
• Maintain healthy body weight (BMI <25)
• Regular physical activity (at least 30 minutes, 5 times a week)
• Avoid smoking and limit alcohol intake
• Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
• Manage stress through relaxation techniques

Pharmacologic Therapy
If lifestyle changes are not enough, 
antihypertensive medications are prescribed:
• Diuretics (e.g., thiazides)
• ACE inhibitors
• Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs)
• Calcium channel blockers
• Beta blockers (in selected cases)

•The goal is to reduce blood 
pressure below 140/90 mmHg, 
or lower for certain patients such 
as diabetics or those with kidney 
disease.

Nursing Management
As nurses, your responsibilities include:
• Accurate BP measurement and documentation
• Monitoring medication compliance and side 
effects
• Educating patients about lifestyle changes and 
the importance of adherence
• Recognizing signs of hypertensive crisis and 
acting promptly
• Coordinating care with physicians and other 
healthcare professionals

Hypertensive Emergency
•A hypertensive emergency is 
when BP exceeds 180/120 
mmHg with target organ 
damage such as chest pain, 
confusion, or renal failure.
This requires immediate 
hospitalization and IV 
antihypertensive therapy.

Conclusion
• In summary, hypertension is a chronic, 
manageable condition that plays a major 
role in cardiovascular morbidity and 
mortality.
Through early detection, lifestyle 
modification, and patient education, 
nurses can significantly reduce the 
complications of hypertension and improve 
patient outcomes.
