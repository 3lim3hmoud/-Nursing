Arteriosclerosis
(hardening of the
arteries) is a general term
for a variety of arterial 
changes.

❖Occurs with aging as degenerative changes
occur in the small arteries and arterioles.
❖Characterized by thickening of the artery walls
that progresses to hardening as calcium 
deposits form Vesselelasticity is lost
❖The thickening and calcification reduce the
diameter of the vessels and cause slowing of 
blood flow, lead to ischemia and necrosis in 
various tissues.

Atherosclerosis form of artery
narrowing.
✓ Lipids are deposited within the
vessel walls and combine with cells,
fibrin, and cell debris to form 
plaques.
✓ The plaque grows and extends into
the lumen of the artery, where
inflammation or erosion causes the 
plaque to be exposed to the blood.
✓ Uncontrolled Diabetes mellitus
&Hypertension is a major factor in
arteriosclerosis

Definition:
• Atherosclerosis is a chronic condition
characterized by the buildup of fatty deposits
(plaques) inside the walls of arteries, leading to
thickening, hardening, and loss of elasticity of
the arterial walls. This process narrows the
arteries, restricting blood flow and potentially
causing cardiovascular diseases such as coronary
artery disease, stroke, and peripheral artery
disease.

Causes:
High cholesterol levels: Elevated 
low-density lipoprotein (LDL) 
cholesterol contributes to plaque 
formation.
Hypertension (high blood pressure):
Damages arterial walls, making them 
more susceptible to plaque buildup.
Smoking: Contributes to inflammation 
and damages blood vessels.
Diabetes mellitus: High blood sugar 
can damage arteries.

Obesity: Increases risk factors such as hypertension 
and diabetes.
Sedentary lifestyle: Lack of exercise leads to poor 
cardiovascular health.
Unhealthy diet: High intake of saturated fats, trans 
fats, and cholesterol.
Age: Risk increases with age.
Genetics: Family history of heart disease or 
atherosclerosis.
Chronic inflammation: From conditions like lupus or 
infections.

Signs and 
Symptoms:
• Atherosclerosis often 
progresses silently until 
significant artery narrowing 
or blockage occurs.

No symptoms in early stages.
• Symptoms depend on affected arteries:
o Coronary arteries: Chest pain (angina), shortness of breath, fatigue.
o Carotid arteries (neck): Sudden weakness, numbness, difficulty speaking, vision 
problems (stroke symptoms).
o Peripheral arteries (legs): Leg pain while walking (claudication), numbness, coldness 
in extremities.
o Severe cases: Heart attack or stroke.

Prevention:
• Healthy diet: Low in saturated fats, trans fats, cholesterol, and sodium; rich in fruits, 
vegetables, whole grains.
• Regular exercise: At least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week.
• Quit smoking: Avoid tobacco in any form.
• Maintain healthy weight: Prevent obesity.
• Control blood pressure and diabetes: Regular monitoring and medication adherence.
• Limit alcohol consumption: Moderate intake.
• Stress management: Techniques like meditation, yoga.

Treatment:
• Lifestyle modifications: Diet, 
exercise, smoking cessation.

Medications:
o Statins (lower cholesterol)
o Antihypertensives (control blood pressure)
o Antiplatelet agents (aspirin) to prevent clot 
formation
o Diabetes management drugs

Surgical interventions:
For severe cases
• Angioplasty with stent 
placement
• Coronary artery bypass 
grafting (CABG)
Regular 
monitoring: Blood 
lipid profiles, 
blood pressure, 
and cardiovascular 
health.

Nursing Care Diagnosis (Examples):
• Ineffective Tissue Perfusion related to arterial occlusion due to atherosclerosis.
• Risk for Decreased Cardiac Output related to coronary artery blockage.
• Activity Intolerance related to decreased oxygen supply to tissues.
• Imbalanced Nutrition: More than Body Requirements related to unhealthy eating habits.
• Risk for Ineffective Health Management related to lack of knowledge about disease and 
treatment.
• Anxiety related to chronic illness and potential complications
