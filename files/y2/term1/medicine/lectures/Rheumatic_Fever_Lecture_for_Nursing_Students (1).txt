Lecture on Rheumatic Fever 
Learning Objectives 
• Define rheumatic fever and its etiology. 
• Describe the pathophysiology and Jones criteria for diagnosis. 
• Identify clinical manifestations and complications. 
• Discuss management, prevention, and nursing care for patients with rheumatic fever. 
Introduction 
Rheumatic fever (RF) is an inflammatory disease that may develop after an infection with 
Group A beta-hemolytic streptococcus (GAS). It primarily affects the heart, joints, skin, and 
central nervous system. The condition is most common among children aged 5–15 years. 
Etiology and Pathophysiology 
Rheumatic fever is a delayed autoimmune response to pharyngeal infection with Group A 
streptococci. Antibodies produced against streptococcal antigens cross-react with human 
tissues, leading to inflammation in the heart (pancarditis), joints, brain, and skin.

Clinical Features 
The clinical presentation of rheumatic fever varies, but major manifestations are 
summarized in the Jones criteria. 
Major Criteria: 
• Carditis 
• Polyarthritis 
• Chorea (Sydenham’s chorea) 
• Erythema marginatum 
• Subcutaneous nodules 
Minor Criteria: 
• Fever 
• Arthralgia 
• Elevated ESR or CRP 
• Prolonged PR interval on ECG

Diagnosis 
Diagnosis of rheumatic fever is based on the revised Jones criteria, which require evidence 
of a preceding streptococcal infection along with the presence of major and minor criteria. 
Management 
Management involves eradication of streptococcal infection, control of inflammation, and 
prevention of recurrence. 
1. Antibiotic therapy: Benzathine penicillin G (single intramuscular injection) is the drug of 
choice. 
2. Anti-inflammatory therapy: Aspirin or corticosteroids to control inflammation. 
3. Supportive care: Bed rest, adequate nutrition, and fluid balance. 
4. Long-term prophylaxis: Monthly benzathine penicillin injections for at least 5–10 years or 
until adulthood. 
Nursing Care 
Nursing management focuses on relieving symptoms, preventing complications, and 
promoting recovery. Key responsibilities include: 
• Monitoring vital signs and cardiac function 
• Administering prescribed medications and antibiotics 
• Educating the patient and family on adherence to prophylaxis 
• Encouraging rest during acute phases 
• Promoting good oral and throat hygiene to prevent infections 
Prevention 
Primary prevention involves prompt treatment of streptococcal throat infections. 
Secondary prevention focuses on long-term antibiotic prophylaxis to prevent recurrence. 
Summary 
Rheumatic fever is a serious post-streptococcal autoimmune condition that can lead to 
chronic rheumatic heart disease if untreated. Early diagnosis, appropriate management, and 
preventive strategies are essential to reduce morbidity among children and adolescents.
