SECTION A — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)

Part 1: Vital Signs & Monitoring 
Q1. What is the target oxygen saturation (SpO2) for a COPD patient on supplemental oxygen? 
A. 94–98% 
B. 88–92%  ✓ ANSWER 
C. 98–100% 
D. 85–88% 
Explanation: COPD patients rely on hypoxic drive. Targeting 88–92% prevents suppression of respiratory drive while 
avoiding hypoxia. 
 
Q2. What is the minimum acceptable Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) in a critically ill patient? 
A. 55 mmHg 
B. 60 mmHg 
C. >65 mmHg  ✓ ANSWER 
D. >80 mmHg 
Explanation: A MAP >65 mmHg is the standard target in sepsis and critical illness to ensure adequate organ 
perfusion. 
 
Q3. A 70 kg patient is admitted to the ICU. What is the expected normal urine output per hour? 
A. 10–20 ml/hr 
B. 35–70 ml/hr  ✓ ANSWER 
C. 100–150 ml/hr 
D. 5–10 ml/hr 
Explanation: Normal urine output is 0.5–1 ml/kg/hr. For 70 kg: 0.5×70 = 35 ml/hr minimum, 1×70 = 70 ml/hr 
maximum.

Q4. A patient opens eyes to voice (3), makes incomprehensible sounds (2), and withdraws from pain 
(4). What is the GCS score? 
A. 7 
B. 8 
C. 9  ✓ ANSWER 
D. 10 
Explanation: GCS = Eye (3) + Verbal (2) + Motor (4) = 9. A score <8 generally indicates need for airway 
protection/mechanical ventilation. 
 
Q5. At what GCS score is endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation typically indicated? 
A. <10 
B. <8  ✓ ANSWER 
C. <12 
D. <6 
Explanation: GCS <8 indicates inability to protect the airway. Intubation is required to prevent aspiration and ensure 
adequate ventilation. 
 
Part 2: Medical Emergencies 
Q6. A patient with DKA is started on insulin. What is the correct initial insulin infusion rate? 
A. 0.05 unit/kg/hr 
B. 0.1 unit/kg/hr  ✓ ANSWER 
C. 0.2 unit/kg/hr 
D. 1 unit/kg/hr 
Explanation: The standard initial insulin infusion in DKA is 0.1 unit/kg/hr as a continuous IV infusion. 
 
Q7. What is the target rate of blood glucose reduction per hour during DKA management? 
A. 10–20 mg/dL/hr 
B. 50–80 mg/dL/hr  ✓ ANSWER 
C. 100–150 mg/dL/hr 
D. >200 mg/dL/hr 
Explanation: Glucose should fall by 50–80 mg/dL/hr. Too rapid a drop risks cerebral edema; too slow suggests 
inadequate treatment. 
 
Q8. Which of the following is the MOST DANGEROUS complication of DKA treatment?

A. Hypernatremia 
B. Cerebral Edema  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Pulmonary Embolism 
D. Hyperkalemia 
Explanation: Cerebral edema is the most feared and potentially fatal complication, especially in children with DKA. It 
results from overly rapid fluid/glucose correction. 
 
Q9. A patient with septic shock has warm extremities, bounding pulse, and low BP. What type of 
shock is this? 
A. Hypovolemic 
B. Cardiogenic 
C. Obstructive 
D. Distributive  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: Septic shock is distributive — massive vasodilation causes low SVR, warm extremities, and hypotension 
despite normal or increased cardiac output. 
 
Q10. A patient suddenly develops hypotension, absent breath sounds on the left, tracheal deviation 
to the right, and jugular venous distension. What is the FIRST action? 
A. Order a chest X-ray 
B. Give IV fluids immediately 
C. Needle decompression of the chest  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Start norepinephrine 
Explanation: This is tension pneumothorax (obstructive shock). Immediate needle decompression (2nd intercostal 
space, midclavicular line) is life-saving — do not wait for imaging. 
 
Q11. What is the FIRST investigation in a patient presenting with acute stroke? 
A. MRI brain with contrast 
B. CT brain WITHOUT contrast  ✓ ANSWER 
C. CT angiography 
D. Lumbar puncture 
Explanation: Non-contrast CT brain is the first-line investigation to differentiate ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke — 
this distinction determines treatment. 
 
Q12. A patient with acute ischemic stroke presents within 48 hours. BP is 185/100 mmHg. What is 
the correct management? 
A. Aggressively lower BP to <140/90 immediately

B. Maintain BP around 180/105 — do not lower aggressively  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Give IV labetalol immediately 
D. No intervention needed for BP 
Explanation: In acute ischemic stroke (first 48 hrs), collateral circulation depends on elevated BP. Aggressive 
lowering extends the infarct. Target ~180/105 unless thrombolytics are used. 
 
Q13. In hemorrhagic stroke, what is the blood pressure target? 
A. <180/120 
B. <160/100 
C. <140/90  ✓ ANSWER 
D. <120/80 
Explanation: In hemorrhagic stroke, the target is BP <140/90 to reduce hematoma expansion, unlike ischemic stroke 
where BP is kept higher. 
 
Q14. Ventricular Fibrillation (V-fib) is identified on the monitor. What is the IMMEDIATE treatment? 
A. IV Amiodarone 
B. IV Atropine 
C. Synchronized DC cardioversion 
D. Unsynchronized DC shock (defibrillation)  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: V-fib is a shockable rhythm requiring immediate unsynchronized defibrillation. Synchronized 
cardioversion is used for organized rhythms (e.g., unstable SVT, VT with pulse). 
 
Q15. DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation) is characterized by: 
A. Only thrombosis 
B. Only bleeding 
C. Thrombosis AND bleeding simultaneously  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Neither thrombosis nor bleeding 
Explanation: DIC paradoxically causes both clotting (microthrombi) and bleeding (consumption of clotting factors 
and platelets) at the same time. 
 
Part 3: Critical Care Conditions 
Q16. A patient with hepatic encephalopathy has elevated ammonia. Which management approach is 
correct? 
A. High protein diet + IV antibiotics 
B. Low protein diet + Lactulose enemas  ✓ ANSWER

C. High protein diet + Lactulose enemas 
D. Nothing by mouth + IV mannitol 
Explanation: Lactulose reduces colonic pH, trapping ammonia as NH4+ and preventing absorption. Low protein diet 
reduces substrate for ammonia production. 
 
Q17. In hypothyroidism, which lab finding is expected? 
A. Low TSH, High T4 
B. High TSH, Low T4  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Low TSH, Low T4 
D. High TSH, High T4 
Explanation: In primary hypothyroidism, the thyroid gland is underactive. The pituitary compensates by secreting 
more TSH. T3/T4 are low while TSH is high. 
 
Q18. A patient has BP of 195/125 mmHg. She is alert, with no headache, chest pain, or neurological 
signs. This is classified as: 
A. Hypertensive Emergency 
B. Hypertensive Urgency  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Normal hypertension 
D. White coat hypertension 
Explanation: Hypertensive Urgency = BP >180/120 WITHOUT acute target organ damage. Management involves 
gradual BP reduction over 24–48 hours, usually with oral agents. 
 
Q19. Which of the following represents a Hypertensive Emergency? 
A. BP 185/110 in a clinic with no symptoms 
B. BP 190/120 with acute pulmonary edema  ✓ ANSWER 
C. BP 178/115 with mild headache 
D. BP 200/100 with anxiety 
Explanation: Hypertensive Emergency = BP >180/120 WITH acute target organ damage (stroke, MI, aortic dissection, 
acute pulmonary edema, acute kidney injury). 
 
Q20. What is the Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) formula? 
A. CPP = MAP + ICP 
B. CPP = MAP - ICP  ✓ ANSWER 
C. CPP = ICP - MAP 
D. CPP = MAP × ICP

Explanation: CPP = MAP – ICP. Target CPP >70 mmHg in head injury. If MAP falls or ICP rises, cerebral perfusion is 
compromised. 
 
Q21. What is the correct head-of-bed elevation for a mechanically ventilated patient to prevent 
VAP? 
A. 0° (flat) 
B. 15–20° 
C. >30–45°  ✓ ANSWER 
D. >60° 
Explanation: Elevating the head of bed >30–45° is the cornerstone of VAP bundle — it reduces aspiration of 
oropharyngeal secretions into the lower respiratory tract. 
 
Q22. A pregnant patient has a BP of 165/108 mmHg with proteinuria and seizures. What is the BP 
target? 
A. <180/120 
B. <160/105  ✓ ANSWER 
C. <120/80 
D. <140/90 
Explanation: In eclampsia, the treatment threshold for antihypertensives is BP >160/105 mmHg. The target is to 
lower BP below this threshold to prevent cerebral complications. 
 
Q23. A patient with suspected acute MI calls from home. While waiting for the ambulance, what 
should he do? 
A. Take metoprolol 50 mg 
B. Chew 4 aspirin tablets (300 mg total)  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Drink water and rest 
D. Take his regular antihypertensive 
Explanation: Aspirin 300 mg (4 × 75 mg tablets) chewed immediately inhibits platelet aggregation and is the first 
critical step in ACS management before hospital arrival. 
 
Q24. Which type of shock is characterized by pump failure with low cardiac output? 
A. Distributive 
B. Obstructive 
C. Hypovolemic 
D. Cardiogenic  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: Cardiogenic shock = heart failure (pump failure). Low CO, high SVR, cold extremities — opposite to 
distributive shock.

Q25. The diagnosis of DKA requires which combination of lab findings? 
A. Blood glucose >250, pH <7.3, Bicarbonate <15  ✓ ANSWER 
B. Blood glucose >180, pH <7.35, Bicarbonate <20 
C. Blood glucose >200, pH <7.1, Bicarbonate <10 
D. Blood glucose >300, pH <7.4, Bicarbonate <22 
Explanation: DKA diagnostic criteria: Blood glucose >250 mg/dL, arterial pH <7.3, and serum bicarbonate <15 mEq/L 
— plus ketonemia/ketonuria. 
 
Part 4: Additional High-Yield Topics 
Q26. In hyperthyroidism (thyrotoxicosis), which lab pattern is expected? 
A. High TSH, High T4 
B. Low TSH, High T4  ✓ ANSWER 
C. High TSH, Low T4 
D. Low TSH, Low T4 
Explanation: In hyperthyroidism, excess thyroid hormones suppress TSH via negative feedback. Result: Low TSH, 
High T3/T4. 
 
Q27. What is the most common complication of stroke in the ICU? 
A. Deep Vein Thrombosis 
B. Aspiration Pneumonia  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Urinary Tract Infection 
D. Pressure sores 
Explanation: Aspiration pneumonia is the most common and serious complication in stroke patients due to impaired 
swallowing and depressed consciousness. 
 
Q28. In ICP management, what head position is recommended? 
A. Flat (0°) 
B. Trendelenburg (head down) 
C. 30–45° elevation, head midline  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Lateral decubitus 
Explanation: Head at 30–45° promotes cerebral venous drainage. Head must be midline (not rotated) to prevent 
jugular compression and ICP spikes.

Q29. Anaphylactic shock is classified as which type of shock? 
A. Hypovolemic 
B. Cardiogenic 
C. Obstructive 
D. Distributive  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: Anaphylaxis causes massive histamine release → systemic vasodilation → distributive shock. 
Management includes IM epinephrine. 
 
Q30. Which intervention is NOT part of the standard VAP (Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia) 
prevention bundle? 
A. Oral care every 4–6 hours 
B. Daily chest X-ray  ✓ ANSWER 
C. DVT prophylaxis 
D. Sedation vacation 
Explanation: The 5-component VAP bundle: HOB elevation, oral care, sedation vacation/weaning, DVT prophylaxis, 
GI stress ulcer prophylaxis. Daily chest X-ray is NOT a bundle component. 
 
Q31. Pulmonary Embolism (PE) causes which type of shock? 
A. Distributive 
B. Hypovolemic 
C. Obstructive  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Cardiogenic 
Explanation: PE causes mechanical obstruction of the pulmonary vasculature → obstructive shock. Hemodynamic 
compromise indicates massive PE. 
 
Q32. Normal urine output in an adult is 0.5–1 ml/kg/hr. For a 60 kg patient, this equals: 
A. 10–20 ml/hr 
B. 30–60 ml/hr  ✓ ANSWER 
C. 60–100 ml/hr 
D. 100–120 ml/hr 
Explanation: 0.5 × 60 = 30 ml/hr (minimum). 1 × 60 = 60 ml/hr (maximum). Below 30 ml/hr = oliguria in this patient. 
 
Q33. In neurogenic shock (a type of distributive shock), which finding is typical? 
A. Tachycardia and hypertension 
B. Bradycardia and hypotension  ✓ ANSWER

C. Tachycardia and hypotension 
D. Bradycardia and normal BP 
Explanation: Neurogenic shock (spinal cord injury) causes loss of sympathetic tone → vasodilation (low BP) + 
unopposed parasympathetic activity → paradoxical bradycardia. 
 
Q34. Which of the following is the BEST indicator of adequate fluid resuscitation in shock? 
A. Heart rate returning to normal 
B. Urine output >0.5 ml/kg/hr 
C. MAP >65 mmHg 
D. All of the above  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: Adequacy of resuscitation is assessed using multiple endpoints: normalization of heart rate, MAP >65 
mmHg, UO >0.5 ml/kg/hr, and improving mental status/lactate. 
 
Q35. What is the target Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) in traumatic brain injury? 
A. CPP >50 mmHg 
B. CPP >60 mmHg 
C. CPP >70 mmHg  ✓ ANSWER 
D. CPP >80 mmHg 
Explanation: CPP >70 mmHg is the target to ensure adequate cerebral blood flow. CPP = MAP – ICP, so maintaining 
high MAP and controlling ICP are both essential. 
 
Q36. A patient in the ICU on a ventilator has fever, new infiltrate on CXR, and purulent tracheal 
secretions 72 hours after intubation. Diagnosis? 
A. Community-acquired pneumonia 
B. Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Pulmonary embolism 
D. Tuberculosis 
Explanation: VAP is defined as pneumonia occurring >48 hours after intubation. New infiltrate + fever + purulent 
secretions in a ventilated patient = VAP until proven otherwise. 
 
Q37. In hypovolemic shock, which set of findings is expected? 
A. Low BP, High HR, Low CVP, Cold extremities  ✓ ANSWER 
B. Low BP, Low HR, High CVP, Warm extremities 
C. High BP, High HR, Low CVP, Cold extremities 
D. Normal BP, High HR, Normal CVP, Warm extremities

Explanation: Hypovolemic shock: hemorrhage/dehydration → Low intravascular volume → Low BP, reflexive 
tachycardia, Low CVP (low preload), cold/clammy extremities (vasoconstriction). 
 
Q38. Daily sedation vacation in mechanically ventilated patients is performed to: 
A. Prevent DVT formation 
B. Assess neurological status and readiness to wean  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Reduce oxygen consumption 
D. Prevent peptic ulcer disease 
Explanation: Sedation vacation allows daily assessment of neurological function and readiness for 
weaning/extubation — a core component of the VAP bundle and liberation from mechanical ventilation. 
 
Q39. Which of the following is an indication to start DVT prophylaxis in an ICU patient? 
A. Only if the patient has a known clot 
B. Routinely for all mechanically ventilated patients  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Only if the patient is ambulatory 
D. Only after surgery 
Explanation: ICU patients are high-risk for DVT due to immobility, critical illness, and central lines. DVT prophylaxis 
(LMWH or compression devices) is a routine VAP bundle component. 
 
Q40. Hypertensive urgency differs from hypertensive emergency in that urgency: 
A. Has higher BP readings 
B. Occurs only in young patients 
C. Has NO acute target organ damage  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Requires IV antihypertensives immediately 
Explanation: The key distinction: Emergency = BP >180/120 WITH organ damage (brain, heart, kidneys). Urgency = 
same BP WITHOUT organ damage. Urgency is managed with oral agents over 24–48 hrs. 
 
Q41. Which finding on DKA workup would most indicate the need for potassium supplementation? 
A. Serum K+ = 5.8 mEq/L 
B. Serum K+ = 4.2 mEq/L 
C. Serum K+ = 3.1 mEq/L  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Serum K+ = 5.2 mEq/L 
Explanation: Despite total body K+ depletion in DKA, serum K+ appears falsely normal/high initially. Insulin therapy 
will drive K+ intracellularly, risking life-threatening hypokalemia — supplement if K+ <3.5.

Q42. A patient has BP 200/130 with acute vision changes, confusion, and rising creatinine. This is 
best described as: 
A. Hypertensive urgency 
B. Hypertensive emergency 
C. Malignant hypertension causing end-organ damage 
D. Both B and C  ✓ ANSWER 
Explanation: Malignant hypertension is a severe form of hypertensive emergency with papilledema and multi -organ 
involvement. It requires immediate IV antihypertensive therapy in ICU. 
 
Q43. Lactulose enemas in hepatic encephalopathy work by: 
A. Decreasing bilirubin levels 
B. Acidifying the colon to trap ammonia as NH4+  ✓ ANSWER 
C. Increasing protein metabolism 
D. Stimulating the liver to regenerate 
Explanation: Lactulose lowers colonic pH → converts NH3 (absorbable) to NH4+ (non-absorbable) → trapped and 
excreted in stool → reduces blood ammonia levels. 
 
Q44. Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia (pVT) should be treated with: 
A. IV Amiodarone alone 
B. CPR only, no electricity 
C. Immediate unsynchronized DC shock  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Synchronized cardioversion 
Explanation: Pulseless VT = shockable rhythm → immediate unsynchronized defibrillation (same as V-fib). 
Synchronized cardioversion is only for organized rhythms WITH a pulse. 
 
Q45. Which complication is most likely to cause death from DKA in a pediatric patient? 
A. Hypoglycemia 
B. Hypokalemia 
C. Cerebral edema  ✓ ANSWER 
D. Aspiration pneumonia 
Explanation: Cerebral edema is the leading cause of DKA-related death in children. It is associated with rapid fluid 
resuscitation and rapid glucose correction. Risk is lower in adults.

SECTION B — Short Answer Questions 
Answer ALL 5 questions. Write concise, structured answers. 
 
• SQ1. List ALL 5 components of the VAP (Ventilator-Associated 
Pneumonia) Prevention Bundle. 
• Elevation of the head of the bed to >30–45 degrees. 
• Oral care (with chlorhexidine solution every 4–6 hours). 
• Daily sedation vacation and assessment for weaning/extubation readiness. 
• DVT (Deep Vein Thrombosis) prophylaxis — LMWH or pneumatic compression 
devices. 
• GIT / Peptic ulcer (stress ulcer) prophylaxis — PPI or H2 blocker. 
 
• SQ2. Describe the key steps in managing a patient with raised 
Intracranial Pressure (ICP). 
• Elevate head of bed to 30–45 degrees. 
• Keep head and neck in a straight, midline position — avoid jugular compression. 
• Target Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) >70 mmHg. CPP = MAP – ICP. Maintain 
MAP and control ICP. 
• Maintain normothermia — fever significantly increases cerebral metabolic demand 
and worsens ICP. 
• Maintain normoglycemia — both hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia worsen 
neurological outcomes. 
• Consider hyperosmolar therapy (Mannitol or Hypertonic Saline) for acute ICP spikes. 
• Avoid hypercapnia — hyperventilation (target PaCO2 35–40 mmHg) can be used 
short-term to reduce ICP. 
 
• SQ3. Differentiate between the FOUR types of shock with one example 
of each. 
• HYPOVOLEMIC — Loss of circulating volume. Cause: hemorrhage (e.g., trauma, GI 
bleed), severe dehydration. 
• CARDIOGENIC — Pump failure; the heart cannot maintain adequate output. Cause: 
massive MI, acute heart failure, cardiomyopathy. 
• OBSTRUCTIVE — Mechanical blockage of blood flow. Cause: Tension Pneumothorax, 
Massive Pulmonary Embolism, Cardiac Tamponade.

• DISTRIBUTIVE — Massive vasodilation causing maldistribution of blood flow. Cause: 
Sepsis (most common), Anaphylaxis, Neurogenic shock. 
 
• SQ4. Outline the management of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) including 
diagnosis, insulin dosing, and key complications. 
• DIAGNOSIS: Random BG >250 mg/dL + pH <7.3 + Bicarbonate <15 mEq/L + 
ketonemia/ketonuria. 
• IV FLUIDS: Start with 0.9% Normal Saline — 1 L over the first hour to restore 
circulating volume. 
• INSULIN: Start continuous IV insulin infusion at 0.1 unit/kg/hr. Target BG reduction 
of 50–80 mg/dL/hr. 
• POTASSIUM: Check serum K+ before starting insulin. Supplement if K+ <3.5 mEq/L. 
Insulin drives K+ intracellularly — monitor closely to prevent hypokalemia. 
• SWITCH TO DEXTROSE: When BG drops to ~250 mg/dL, switch IV fluids to D5W + 
0.45% NaCl to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin to clear ketones. 
• KEY COMPLICATIONS: Hypoglycemia, Hypokalemia (life-threatening arrhythmias), 
Cerebral Edema (leading cause of death especially in children — from rapid 
fluid/glucose correction). 
 
• SQ5. Compare and contrast the blood pressure management in 
ISCHEMIC vs. HEMORRHAGIC stroke. 
• FIRST STEP FOR BOTH: Non-contrast CT brain to differentiate hemorrhagic from 
ischemic stroke. 
• ISCHEMIC STROKE (first 48 hours): DO NOT lower BP aggressively. Target BP 
~180/105 mmHg. The elevated BP is compensatory, maintaining collateral flow to 
the ischemic penumbra. Exception: if IV thrombolytics (tPA) are given, lower BP to 
<185/110 before administration. 
• HEMORRHAGIC STROKE: Lower BP more aggressively. Target systolic BP <140 mmHg 
to reduce hematoma expansion and re-bleeding risk. 
• MAJOR COMPLICATION of STROKE: Aspiration Pneumonia — due to impaired gag 
reflex and dysphagia. 
• ECLAMPSIA (special case): Treat BP >160/105 mmHg with IV labetalol or hydralazine. 
Magnesium sulfate for seizure prevention.
