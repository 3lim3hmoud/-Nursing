• Sinus Dysrhythmias
• 1-Sinus Tachycardia

Sinus tachycardia
• Sinus tachycardia originates in the SA 
node & discharges impulses regularly at 
a rate between 100 – 160 times/minute.  
• Sinus tachycardia meets all the criteria 
for normal sinus rhythm except that  the 
rate is greater than 100 beat/min.
• It is a normal response to stimulation of 
the sympathetic nervous system,  also 
is a normal findings in children under 
age of 6 years

ECG 
Characteristics 
of Sinus 
Tachycardia
• Rhythm:    Atrial and Ventricle Rhythm are regular 
• Rate:  Atrial and Ventricle  rate are greater than 100 
beat/minute ,
• P wave: normal size and configuration. 
• Precedes each QRS, duration from 0.06 to less than 
0.12 second. 
• P Interval: within normal limit. (0.12 to 0.20 second) 
• QRS Complex: Normal duration and configuration.
• Duration from 0.06 to 0.10 second in width.  
• T wave: Normal size and configuration. 
• QT Interval: within normal limit, but commonly 
shorted 
• (N.B) All other ECG characteristics, except for heart 
rate, are the same as in normal sinus rhythm.

Causes of 
Sinus 
Tachycardia
• All normal responses to stimulation 
of the sympathetic nervous system:
Exercise, pain, stress, fear, anxiety, 
exercise, excessive coffee. 
• *Cardiac condition: Heart failure, 
cardiogenic shock, pericarditis. 
• *Other condition: Hyperdynamic 
circulation, shock, anemia, 
respiratory distress, pulmonary 
embolism, hyperthyroidism. 
• *Drugs: Atropine and dopamine 
• *Alcohol 
• Nicotine

Signs and 
Symptoms of 
Sinus 
Tachycardia
The patient will have a peripheral pulse rate 
above 100 beat/minute, but with regular 
rhythm (asymptomatic) 
Sinus Tachycardia is detrimental in ischemic 
heart diseases patient because it decreases 
the time for ventricular filling, decreases stroke 
volume & thus compromises cardiac output.  
Sinus tachycardia is often the earliest sign of 
Left Ventricular Failure (LVF), shock, or infarct 
extension.

Treatment of 
Sinus 
Tachycardia:
Treatment Goals
*Maintain adequate cardiac 
output *Maintain adequate 
tissue perfusion 
*Correct the underlying cause.

• 2-Sinus Bradycardia
• Sinus bradycardia results when the SA node generates fewer 
than 60 
• beat/minute

Signs and Symptoms of Sinus Bradycardia
*Asymptomatic bradycardia means no signs 
and symptoms of cardiac output appear  
* Symptomatic bradycardia occurs when 
manifestations of low cardiac output appear 
such as: Hypotension, cold-clammy skin, 
dizziness, blurred vision, altered mental 
status, chest pain, syncope, crackles, 
dyspnea &S3.

Treatment 
of Sinus 
Bradycardia
Sinus bradycardia usually does 
not require treatment unless the 
patient displays symptoms of 
hypoperfusion,
such as hypotension, dizziness, 
chest pain, or changes in level of 
consciousness. -No treatment is required until 
symptom of low cardiac output 
appears. -Correct the underline causes 
-Administer drug such as: 
Atropine, Dopamine, Epinephrine

Atrial Flutter
1-Atrial Flutter

Mechanism of Atrial flutter 
• Atrial flutter is a condition in which 
there is an abnormal focus in the 
atrium that discharges rapid regular 
impulses but due to the protective 
mechanism in the AV node, which does 
not allow all of the atrial impulses to be 
conducted to the ventricles.

Characteristic 
s of Atrial
Flutter ECG 
Rhythm 
Rhythm :  Atrial rhythm is regular  
Ventricular rhythm regular or may 
be irregular depend   on the AV 
conduction pattern  Rate: Atrial rate 250-350 b/min 
P Wave: replaced by flutter  (saw 
tooth)QRS Complex: exhibits a normal 
configuration PR interval: Cannot be measured. 
P: QRS Ratio: Many: 1

Signs and 
symptoms 
of Atrial 
flutter 
* Usually the patient is 
asymptomatic unless atrial flutter 
result in a tachycardia called 
Rapid Ventricular Response (RVR) 
that occurs when the ventricular 
response is greater than 100 
beat/min. *If Atrial Flutter occurs in 
conjunction with a rapid 
ventricular rate, the ventricular 
chambers cannot fill adequately, 
resulting in varying degrees of 
hemodynamic compromise

Causes of 
Atrial Flutter 
Atrial flutter often is seen in the 
presence of underlying cardiac 
disease, including 
Coronary Artery Diseases (CAD), 
Cor-pulmonale, and rheumatic 
heart disease. 
Hypoxemia and alcoholism can 
cause Atrial Flutter.

• Antidysrhythmic 
Medications 
• prescribed for two reasons: 
*Convert the atrial flutter to 
sinus rhythm (rhythm 
control) and 
• *Slow conduction through 
the AV node (rate control). 
• The most effective 
medications for rhythm 
control are Amiodarone, and 
Sotalol.

Atrial fibrillation 
• Definition: Atrial fibrillation (AF or A-fib) is 
an abnormal heart rhythm characterized 
by rapid and irregular beating of the atria 
often it starts as brief periods of abnormal 
beating which become longer and 
possibly constant over time

Causes of Atrial fibrillation 
• Cardiovascular factors known to be 
associated with the development of AF 
include: 
• High blood pressure,  
• Coronary artery disease,  
• Heart valve disease 
• Left atrial enlargement,  
• hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), 
• Pericarditis,

Signs and 
Symptoms 
of Atrial 
Fibrillation 
Rapid and irregular heart rates 
may be perceived as 
The sensation of the heart beating 
too fast, irregularly, or skipping 
beats (palpitations), tiredness, 
shortness of breath, dizziness or 
feeling faint or exercise intolerance and 
occasionally may produce anginal 
chest pain (if the high heart rate 
causes the heart's’demand for 
oxygen to increase beyond the 
supply of available oxygen 
(ischemia)

Treatment 
of Atrial 
Fibrillation 
The acute management of AF can be divided 
into 3 components: 
Control of the heart rate, 
Adenosine 
Cardioversion (electrical and pharmacological 
), 
Thromboprophylaxis.

Electrical Cardioversion 
• More  than  50%  of  episodes  of  recent-onset  AF  
will   spontaneously  revert  to  sinus  rhythm within  
the  first  72  hours .  
• For  the  remaining  cases  of  AF  that  are  
complicated  by hypotension,  pulmonary  edema,  
or  myocardial   ischemia,  direct-current  
cardioversion  is the  appropriate  intervention.  
• An  energy  of  100  J  is  usually  enough  for  
successful   cardioversion  using  biphasic  shocks, 
but  200  J  is  recommended  for  the  initial   
cardioversion  attempt  in  the  most  recent 
guidelines  on  AF .

Pharmacological Cardioversion 
• Drug-induced  cardioversion  is  used  in  cases  of  uncomplicated  
AF  that  are  refractory  to rate  control ,  or  for  first  episodes  of  
uncomplicated  AF  that  are  less  than  48  hours  in duration,  to  
eliminate  the  need  for  anti coagulation  . 
• Several   antiarrhythmic agents  can  be  effective  in  terminating  
AF ,  including  amiodarone  .

Ventricular 
Dysrhythmias  
• 1-Premature Ventricular Contractions (PVC)s
• An ectopic beat originating prematurely at the 
level of the ventricles. 
•  The beat is ventricular in origin and results in no 
electrical activity in the  atria. As a result, no P 
waves appear.

1-Premature Ventricular 
Contractions (PVC)s
• Rhythm:    Irregular 
• Rate:  Underlying rhythm 
• P wave: None with PVC but P wave present 
with Other QRS complex. 
• PR Interval: Not measurable  QRS Complex: 
Wide ORS  
• T wave: opposite direction from QRS 
• Others: may be followed by compensatory 
pause

Causes of Premature Ventricular Contractions 
• The most common of all ectopic beats, PVCs can occur with or 
without heart disease in any age group. They are especially 
common in people with myocardial disease (ischemia or 
infarction) or with myocardial irritability (hypokalemia, increased 
levels of catecholamines, or mechanical irritation with a wire or 
catheter).

Treatment of Premature Ventricular Contractions 
Infrequent, isolated 
PVCs require no 
treatment.  
Multiple or 
consecutive PVCs 
may be managed with 
antiarrhythmic agents. 
In the emergency 
setting, Amiodarone 
and Lidocaine are the 
drugs of choice.  
If serum potassium is 
low, potassium 
replacement may 
correct the 
dysrhythmia.  
If the dysrhythmia is 
caused by Digitalis 
toxicity, withdrawal of 
the  Digitalis may 
correct it.

Ventricular 
Tachycardia 
Characteristic of Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) 
Rhythm:   Ventricular rhythm regular 
Rate:       Rapid rate over 100 beat/min 
QRS shape and duration:    Greater than 0.12 seconds, bizarre, 
abnormal shape.(wide ORS complex) 
P wave: absent 
PR Interval: Not measurable 
• T wave: opposite direction from QRS complex

Causes of 
Ventricular 
Tachycardia 
Commonly occurs in myocardial 
ischemia or Infarction  
Cardiomyopathy  
Mitral Valve Prolapse  
Congestive Heart Failure  
Digitalis toxicity is a common cause of 
VT

Signs and 
Symptoms 
of 
Ventricular 
Tachycardia 
• Signs and symptoms of hemodynamic 
compromise (e.g., ischemic chest pain, 
hypotension, pulmonary edema, and 
unconsciousness) may be seen if the rate 
is fast and the tachycardia is sustained.

Treatment 
of 
Ventricular 
Tachycardia 
• If the patient is hemodynamically stable 
with the dysrhythmia, Lidocaine may be 
administered intravenously. 
• If the patient becomes unstable, 
synchronized cardio version (or 
unsynchronized defibrillation in 
emergency situations) is indicated. 
• Long-term treatment for this dysrhythmia 
may involve the use of an Implantable 
Cardioverter Defibrillator (ICD).
