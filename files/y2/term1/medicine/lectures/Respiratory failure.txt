Introduction
Respiratory failure is a life-threatening condition 
in which the respiratory system fails in one or 
both of its gas-exchange functions:
• Oxygenation (oxygen delivery to the blood)
• Ventilation (removal of carbon dioxide)
It can occur acutely (minutes to hours) or 
chronically (days to years).

1. Type I — Hypoxemic Respiratory Failure

A. Based on Gas Exchange Abnormalities
Characterized by:-
• PaO₂ < 60 mmHg
• Normal or low PaCO₂
Caused by:-
problems primarily with oxygenation.
Mechanisms:
• V/Q mismatch
• Shunt
• Diffusion impairment
• Low inspired oxygen

Common causes:
• Pneumonia
• Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
• Pulmonary edema
• Pulmonary embolism
• Interstitial lung diseases

2. Type II — Hypercapnic Respiratory 
Failure
Characterized by:-
• PaCO₂ > 45 mmHg
• Often accompanied by acidemia (low pH)
Caused by:-
• hypoventilation (insufficient CO₂ removal).

Mechanisms:
• Decreased respiratory drive
• Airflow obstruction
• Neuromuscular weakness
• Chest wall abnormalities

Common causes:
• COPD exacerbation
• Asthma severe attack
• Drug overdose (opioids, sedatives)
• Neuromuscular disorders (myasthenia gravis, 
Guillain-Barré)
• Obesity hypoventilation syndrome

3. Type III — Perioperative Respiratory 
Failure
• Due to atelectasis usually after surgery.
• Occurs commonly after abdominal or thoracic 
surgery.

4. Type IV — Shock-related Respiratory 
Failure
• Associated with hypoperfusion, usually in 
shock.
• Respiratory muscles fatigue due to inadequate 
oxygen delivery.

A. V/Q Mismatch
• Most common mechanism.
• Portions of the lung receive oxygen but not 
enough blood, or vice versa.
• Improves with supplemental O₂.

B. Shunt
• Blood bypasses ventilated alveoli entirely.
• Does NOT improve significantly with 
oxygen therapy.
Causes: 
• pneumonia, 
• ARDS, 
• atelectasis.

C. Diffusion Limitation
• Thickened alveolar membrane prevents 
efficient diffusion.
• Seen in interstitial lung diseases.

D. Hypoventilation
• CO₂ accumulation → hypercapnia.
• Causes include sedation, neuromuscular 
impairment, or chest wall restriction.

Symptoms:
• Dyspnea
• Tachypnea
• Use of accessory muscles
• Confusion or agitation (hypercapnia or 
hypoxemia)
• Cyanosis
• Fatigue and inability to speak in full sentences

Signs of impending respiratory arrest:
• Altered mental status
• Bradypnea or irregular breathing
• Silent chest (severe airway obstruction)
• Poor respiratory effort

A. Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)
• Confirms hypoxemia, hypercapnia, and 
acid–base balance.
• Essential for classifying the type of 
respiratory failure.

B. Chest Imaging
Chest X-ray or CT to identify pneumonia, edema, 
pneumothorax, or ARDS.
C. Pulse Oximetry
• Quick assessment of oxygen saturation.
D. Additional Tests
• CBC, electrolytes
• ECG (evaluate for cardiac causes)
• Pulmonary function tests (for chronic cases)

6. Management Principles
Always address:
• 1-Airway
• 2-Breathing
• 3-Circulation
A. General Support
• Oxygen therapy to maintain adequate saturation
• Treat underlying cause (infection, fluid overload, 
bronchospasm, PE)

B. Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV)
Useful in:
1-COPD exacerbations
2-Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
3-Select neuromuscular patients
Avoid if:
1-Severe acidosis
2-Hemodynamic instability
3- Inability to protect airw

C. Invasive Mechanical Ventilation
Indicated when:
• NIV fails
• Airway protection is needed
• Severe hypoxemia or hypercapnia with acidemia
• Modes could include volume control, pressure support, 
etc.

D. Treating Underlying Causes
• Antibiotics for pneumonia
• Bronchodilators + steroids for COPD/asthma
• Diuretics for pulmonary edema
• Anticoagulation for pulmonary embolism
• Reversal of drug overdose (e.g., naloxone)

7. Complications
• Barotrauma (pneumothorax)
• Ventilator-associated pneumonia
• Hypoxic organ damage
• Cardiac arrhythmias
• Respiratory acidosis/alkalosis

8. Prognosis
Depends on:
• Cause (e.g., COPD vs ARDS)
• Timeliness of intervention
• Presence of multi-organ failure
• Age and comorbidities
• ARDS and sepsis-related respiratory failure have the 
highest mortality.
