Tips for Filling Out a Nursing Report Sheet Efficiently
1- Use Abbreviations & Symbols.
2- Focus on What’s Important.
3- Keep It Legible & Organized.
4- Update as You Go.
5- Complete the sheet comprehensively and accurately.
6- Use clear and concise language.
7- Document all relevant observations and concerns.
8- Proofread the sheet carefully before handing off to the next nurse.
9- Ask questions and clarify any uncertainties.
10- Utilize standardized abbreviations and terminology..

Case study # 01
Sarah Johnson, a 37-year-old woman, was admitted to room 204B during 
the morning shift on October 17, 2025. She has a medical history of 
asthma and seasonal allergies and previously had an appendectomy in 
2010.
Sarah was diagnosed with community-acquired pneumonia. Her code 
status is G312, and she has no isolation precautions. She is allergic to 
penicillin.
At the time of assessment, her temperature was 38.2°C, heart rate 96 bpm, 
blood pressure 118/74 mmHg, respiratory rate 20 breaths per minute, and 
oxygen saturation 94% on room air. She reported mild chest pain (3 out of 
10), which was managed with Tylenol.
A peripheral IV line was placed in her left forearm. Her breathing was 
labored, so she was placed on 2 liters of oxygen via nasal cannula. Her 
cardiac rhythm was regular, bowel and bladder function were normal, and 
her skin was intact..

Case study # 02
Liam Carter, a 9-year-old boy, was admitted to the pediatric unit in room 112A during the 
evening shift on October 17, 2025. His diagnosis was Influenza B, and he was placed 
under droplet isolation precautions. Liam has a past medical history of mild intermittent 
asthma, but no history of surgeries. He has no known allergies.
At the time of assessment, Liam’s temperature was 38.5°C, heart rate 110 bpm, blood 
pressure 100/60 mmHg, respiratory rate 22 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation 97% 
on room air. He complained of a headache with a pain level of 4 out of 10, which was 
treated with Ibuprofen.
He had a peripheral IV line inserted in his right hand for medication administration. His 
neurological, cardiac, respiratory, gastrointestinal, and genitourinary assessments were all 
normal, and his skin was intact. A rapid flu test came back positive for Influenza B.
Liam’s current medications included Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) 45 mg twice daily, Albuterol 
nebulizer every 6 hours as needed for wheezing, and Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg every 6 hours as 
needed for fever. The physician advised to continue Tamiflu and provide supportive care.
Nursing interventions included encouraging fluids, monitoring temperature, and 
maintaining isolation precautions. Liam’s mother was concerned about his decreased 
appetite. The nursing plan emphasized symptom control, hydration, and infection control. 
Liam was expected to be discharged once he was afebrile for 24 hours and eating well.
