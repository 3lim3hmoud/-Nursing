What is Hyperthyroidism? 
• A condition where the thyroid gland produces excess thyroid 
hormones (T3 and T4). 
• Leads to a hypermetabolic state affecting multiple body 
systems. 
• Affects about 1-2% of the population, more common in 
women (5-10x). 
Thyroid Physiology: The Hormonal Feedback Loop 
• Hypothalamus releases TRH → stimulates pituitary to 
release TSH. 
• TSH stimulates thyroid gland to produce T3 (active) and T4 
hormones. 
• Negative feedback: high T3/T4 suppress TRH and TSH 
release. 
• Thyroid hormones regulate metabolism, growth, heart rate, 
and temperature. 
Major Causes of Hyperthyroidism 
Graves’ Disease (60-80% of cases): Autoimmune stimulation of 
TSH receptors by antibodies. 
Toxic Multinodular Goiter: Multiple thyroid nodules 
autonomously producing hormones. 
Toxic Adenoma: Single overactive thyroid nodule. 
Other causes: Thyroiditis, iodine excess, medication-induced 
(e.g., amiodarone). 
Clinical Symptoms and Signs 
Common symptoms: weight loss despite appetite, heat 
intolerance, sweating, palpitations, anxiety, tremors. 
Physical signs: diffuse goiter, tachycardia, exophthalmos 
(bulging eyes in Graves’), pretibial myxedema. 
Variable presentation: some patients may have subtle or no 
symptoms initially. 
Diagnostic Approach

Blood tests: suppressed TSH with elevated free T4 and/or T3 
confirms hyperthyroidism. 
Antibody testing: TSH receptor antibodies confirm Graves’ 
disease. 
Imaging: thyroid ultrasound and radioactive iodine uptake scan 
to identify nodules or inflammation. 
Treatment Options 
Antithyroid medications (methimazole, propylthiouracil) to block 
hormone synthesis. 
Radioactive iodine therapy to ablate overactive thyroid tissue 
(most common in the US). 
Surgery (thyroidectomy) for large goiters, nodules, or when 
other treatments fail. 
Beta-blockers to control symptoms like rapid heart rate and 
tremors. 
Special Considerations 
Thyroid storm: life-threatening exacerbation with fever, 
tachycardia, delirium; requires emergency care. 
Pregnancy: careful management needed; some antithyroid 
drugs are preferred. 
Monitoring for hypothyroidism after treatment is essential. 
Case Highlight: Graves’ Disease 
Autoimmune disorder causing diffuse thyroid enlargement and 
eye disease. 
TSH receptor antibodies mimic TSH → excessive hormone 
production. 
Up to 30% develop Graves’ ophthalmopathy with eye bulging 
and discomfort.

Requires multidisciplinary care including endocrinology and 
ophthalmology. 
Summary & Takeaway 
Hyperthyroidism is a common, treatable endocrine disorder with 
systemic effects. 
Early diagnosis and tailored treatment prevent complications. 
Understanding causes and symptoms helps improve patient 
outcomes. 
Stay vigilant for thyroid storm and special populations like 
pregnant women.
