B A C K G R O U N D
Benjamin S. Bloom & the Origins
How a 1948 committee meeting changed educational theory forever
BSB
Benjamin S. Bloom
1913 – 1999
Psychologist & Educator
University of Chicago
1948
American Psychological Association (APA) conference — Bloom proposes a 
classification system for educational objectives.
1956
Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: Cognitive Domain published — the 
original 6-level hierarchy revolutionizes curriculum design.
1964
Krathwohl, Bloom & Masia publish the Affective Domain taxonomy, 
addressing attitudes and values.
2001
Anderson & Krathwohl revise the taxonomy — renaming levels with verbs, 
moving Synthesis→Create to the top.

O R I G I N A L  T A X O N O M Y
The Original Taxonomy — 1956
Six levels in the Cognitive Domain — arranged from lower to higher order thinking
1 Knowledge
Recall of facts, terms, and basic concepts
e.g. List the 5 stages of cell division
2 Comprehension
Explain or interpret meaning from 
material
e.g. Explain how photosynthesis works
3 Application
Use information in new and concrete 
situations
e.g. Solve a chemistry equation
4 Analysis
Break material into parts; find patterns
e.g. Compare the causes of WWI vs 
WWII
5 Synthesis
Combine elements to form a new whole
e.g. Design an experiment to test a 
hypothesis
6 Evaluation
Judge value; justify decisions or courses 
of action
e.g. Assess the validity of a research 
paper

R E V I S E D  T A X O N O M Y  2 0 0 1
The Revised Taxonomy — 2001
Anderson & Krathwohl — nouns replaced by verbs, Synthesis renamed Create & moved to the apex
1 Remember
Retrieve relevant knowledge from 
memory
e.g. Name the bones of the human hand
2 Understand
Construct meaning from instructional 
messages
e.g. Summarize a research article in own 
words
3 Apply
Carry out a procedure in a given 
situation
e.g. Calculate medication dosage for a 
patient
4 Analyze
Break material into component parts; 
determine how they interrelate
e.g. Differentiate between Type 1 and 
Type 2 diabetes
5 Evaluate
Make judgments based on criteria and 
standards
e.g. Critique the methodology of a 
clinical trial
6 Create
Put elements together to form a novel 
whole
e.g. Design a patient care protocol from 
scratch

C O M P A R I S O N
Original vs. Revised: What Changed?
The 2001 revision transformed nouns into action verbs and restructured the apex
Original (1956) Revised (2001) Key Change
Knowledge Remember Shifted focus from noun (content) to verb (action)
Comprehension Understand More active — learners construct meaning
Application Apply Verb form clarifies observable behavior
Analysis Analyze Consistent verb convention
Synthesis Create Renamed AND moved to top — highest cognitive demand
Evaluation Evaluate Now Level 5, below Create (swapped order)

C O G N I T I V E  D O M A I N
Cognitive Domain: Verbs & Examples
Mental skills — from simple recall to complex creation. Use these verbs when writing learning objectives.
L1 Remember
Verbs: Define, List, Recall, Name, Identify, Label, Repeat, State
Example: List the 4 blood types
L2 Understand
Verbs: Explain, Summarize, Paraphrase, Classify, Describe, Interpret, 
Compare
Example: Explain the water cycle
L3 Apply
Verbs: Solve, Demonstrate, Calculate, Use, Execute, Implement, Operate
Example: Calculate medication dosage
L4 Analyze
Verbs: Compare, Differentiate, Examine, Break down, Distinguish, Organize
Example: Compare WWI & WWII causes
L5 Evaluate
Verbs: Judge, Justify, Critique, Defend, Assess, Argue, Prioritize
Example: Critique a research methodology
L6 Create
Verbs: Design, Construct, Formulate, Develop, Compose, Plan, Produce
Example: Design a new patient care protocol

P S Y C H O M O T O R  D O M A I N
Psychomotor Domain: Verbs & Examples
Physical skills — motor coordination from imitation to mastery (Simpson, 1972)
L1 Perception
Verbs: Detect, Choose, Identify, Distinguish
Ex: Student notices incorrect grip on surgical tool
L2 Set
Verbs: Prepare, Position, Display, Begin, React
Ex: Positions hands correctly before starting IV insertion
L3 Guided Response
Verbs: Copy, Follow, Imitate, Attempt, Trace
Ex: Follows step-by-step guide to suture a wound
L4 Mechanism
Verbs: Calibrate, Construct, Operate, Perform
Ex: Operates ECG machine without supervision
L5 Complex Response
Verbs: Execute, Adapt, Coordinate, Refine, Automate
Ex: Performs laparoscopic procedure with full fluency

A F F E C T I V E  D O M A I N
Affective Domain: Verbs & Examples
Attitudes, values & emotions — from passive awareness to internalized belief (Krathwohl et al., 1964)
L1 Receiving
Verbs: Listen, Attend, Notice, Accept, Be aware
Ex: Student listens attentively to a patient's concerns
L2 Responding
Verbs: Participate, Comply, Discuss, Contribute, React
Ex: Actively participates in ethics seminar discussion
L3 Valuing
Verbs: Appreciate, Prefer, Justify, Support, Advocate
Ex: Advocates for patient rights in team meetings
L4 Organization
Verbs: Organize, Prioritize, Integrate, Balance, Compare
Ex: Reconciles personal beliefs with professional ethics
L5 Characterization
Verbs: Internalize, Embody, Demonstrate, Revise, Develop
Ex: Consistently embodies patient-centered care values

G O A L S  &  O B J E C T I V E S
Formulating Goals & Objectives
Understanding the critical distinction between broad goals and precise, measurable objectives
G O A L S
Broad Intent
General, long-term statements of educational purpose
NOT directly measurable or observable
Express ideals, aspirations, and values
Apply to a course, program, or institution
Guide the overall direction of instruction
Example: "Students will develop an appreciation for evidence-based 
practice."
O B J E C T I V E S
Specific Intent
Specific, measurable statements of what learners DO
Observable behaviors — can be seen or evaluated
Include a clear standard or criterion for success
Apply at the lesson, unit, or session level
Drive assessment design and teaching strategies
Example: "Given a patient case, students will calculate oral 
medication dose with 90% accuracy."

A B C D  M O D E L
The ABCD Model for Writing Objectives
Four essential components that make a learning objective complete and unambiguous
A
Audience
WHO are the learners?
Identify the specific group being 
taught. The objective describes what 
THEY will do, not what the teacher 
will teach. Always make the learner 
the subject.
Nursing students
Medical interns
Grade 6 pupils
B
Behavior
What will they DO?
Use an observable ACTION VERB 
from Bloom's Taxonomy. Must be 
something that can be seen, heard, 
or evaluated. Avoid vague verbs like 
'know', 'understand', or 'appreciate'.
Identify
Calculate
Construct
C
Condition
Under what CIRCUMSTANCES?
Specify the context, tools, materials, 
or constraints given to the learner 
when performing the task.
Given a patient case study...
Without reference materials...
Using a standard calculator...
D
Degree
How WELL must they perform?
Define the minimum acceptable 
standard — the criterion that 
separates success from failure. This 
makes the objective measurable.
...with 80% accuracy
...in 4 out of 5 cases
...in under 3 minutes

A B C D  E X A M P L E S
ABCD in Practice: Worked Examples
Three complete objectives across different disciplines — each component labeled
Healthcare / Nursing
A First-year nursing students B will correctly measure and document vital signs
C given a standardized simulated patient scenario D with 90% accuracy across 5 consecutive assessments
Science Education
A Grade 10 biology students B will construct and label a detailed diagram of cell division
C without the use of textbooks or notes D correctly identifying all 5 phases with ≥80% accuracy
Pharmacy Training
A Third-year pharmacy students B will calculate and verify the appropriate drug dosage
C given a laboratory practical exam with 10 patient scenarios D achieving a passing score of 9 out of 10 cases
A = Audience B = Behavior C = Condition D = Degree

O B J E C T I V E  Q U A L I T Y
Weak vs. Strong Objectives
Spot the difference — and fix it. This is the most practical skill in instructional design.
WEAK
"Students will know about infection control." =>
STRONG
Psychomotor —
Mechanism
"Given a clinical simulation, nursing students will demonstrate correct 
hand hygiene technique before and after patient contact in 5 out of 5 
observations."
WEAK
"Learners will understand cardiovascular disease." =>
STRONG Cognitive — Analyze
"After the cardiology lecture, students will compare the 
pathophysiology of atherosclerosis vs. hypertension using a Venn 
diagram with at least 4 correct distinctions."
WEAK
"Students will appreciate ethical practice." =>
STRONG Affective — Valuing
"Following the bioethics workshop, students will defend their chosen 
position on a given case study in a written reflection of 300+ words, 
citing at least 2 ethical principles."

I N T E G R A T I O N
ABCD + SMART: Two Frameworks, One Objective
ABCD gives you the structure; SMART gives you the quality check. Use both together.
C O M P L E T E  O B J E C T I V E :
A Second-year pharmacy students B will correctly calculate drug dosage
C given a practical exam with patient scenarios D achieving 9 of 10 correct.
ABCD Checklist
A Audience: 'Second-year pharmacy students'
B Behavior: 'will correctly calculate dosage'
C Condition: 'given a practical exam...'
D Degree: 'achieving 9 out of 10'
SMART Checklist
S Specific: 'Calculate dosage' — clear action verb
M Measurable: '9 out of 10' — quantified criterion
A Achievable: Appropriate for 2nd-year level
R Relevant: Core pharmacy competency
T Time-bound: 'Given a practical exam' — defined event

Key Takeaways
1 Three Domains —
All learning is Cognitive (thinking), Psychomotor (doing), or Affective (feeling). Effective 
design addresses all three.
2 Hierarchy Matters —
Each domain progresses from lower to higher order. Build foundational objectives before 
advanced ones.
3 Verb Choice is Critical —
Action verbs define the observable behavior and the domain level. 'Design' signals Create 
(L6); 'List' signals Remember (L1).
4 Goals ≠ Objectives —
Goals are broad ideals; objectives are specific, observable, measurable statements of 
learner behavior.
5 ABCD Model —
Every strong objective has: Audience, Behavior, Condition, and Degree. Missing any 
component weakens it.
6 SMART + ABCD = Excellence —
ABCD provides structure; SMART ensures quality. Apply both frameworks to every 
objective you write.
