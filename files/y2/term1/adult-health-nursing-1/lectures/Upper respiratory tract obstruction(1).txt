- Upper respiratory tract 
obstruction refers to a partial or 
complete blockage of the airway 
above the vocal cords (larynx), 
including the nose, pharynx, and 
larynx, which can interfere with 
normal breathing.
-This can be acute (sudden 
onset, potentially life-
threatening) or chronic 
(develops over time).

Pathophysiology of Upper Respiratory Tract Obstruction:
Anatomical narrowing 
or blockage due to 
swelling, mass, foreign 
body, trauma, or 
infection.
Any obstruction 
increases the 
resistance to airflow, 
particularly during 
inspiration 
Obstruction can prevent 
adequate oxygen from 
reaching the alveoli, 
leading to hypoxia
High-pitched, noisy breathing 
(stridor) often results from 
turbulent airflow through a 
narrowed upper airway.
Prolonged obstruction leads to 
fatigue, respiratory failure, 
and potentially cardiac arrest.

Causes
1- Infections
o Tonsillitis, pharyngitis 
(severe swelling)
o Epiglottitis (inflammation of 
the epiglottis)
o Laryngitis
2- Trauma
o Facial or neck injuries
o Inhalation injury (burns, 
smoke inhalation)
o Foreign body aspiration
3- Congenital abnormalities
o Choanal atresia
o Laryngomalacia

4- Neoplasms
Tumors or growths in 
the airway (benign or 
malignant)
5- Allergic reactions
Angioedema or 
anaphylaxis causing 
swelling of airway 
structures
6- Foreign bodies
Objects lodged in the 
airway, especially 
common in children

7- Obstructive sleep apnea
o Soft tissue 
collapses in the 
pharynx during 
sleep
8- Neurological 
conditions
o V ocal cord 
paralysis
o Reduced 
airway tone 
causing 
collapse

Symptoms
Stridor Dyspnea Cough Cyanosis 
Hoarseness Difficulty 
swallowing Restlessness Tachypnea

Risk Factors
Age
Neurological 
disorders
Trauma or 
surgery
Use of certain 
medications
Exposure to 
irritants
Recent upper 
respiratory 
infection
History of 
allergies or 
asthma

Types of Upper Respiratory Tract Obstruction:
1. Acute Obstruction
• Sudden onset; requires urgent management.
Causes:
o Foreign body aspiration (common in children)
o Acute allergic reaction (anaphylaxis → laryngeal edema)
o Infections (e.g., epiglottitis, croup, peritonsillar abscess)
o Trauma (e.g., facial fractures, burns, laryngeal injury)

2. Chronic Obstruction
Tumors (laryngeal, 
nasopharyngeal) V ocal cord paralysis
Enlarged tonsils Congenital 
abnormalities

3. Partial Obstruction
Airflow is reduced but still 
present.
Symptoms:  hoarseness, 
dyspnea, cough.
4. Complete Obstruction
Total blockage of airflow; a 
medical emergency.
Symptoms: Inability to speak or 
breathe, cyanosis, loss of 
consciousness.
5. Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic 
Obstruction
Intrinsic: Caused by something 
within the airway (e.g., tumor).
Extrinsic: Caused by external 
compression (e.g., thyroid mass 
pressing on trachea).

Respiratory Tract Infection 
Respiratory Tract Infection refers 
to infections that affect the 
respiratory tract, which includes 
the nose, throat, airways, and 
lungs.

Upper Respiratory Tract 
Infection (URTI): Involves 
nose, sinuses, pharynx, larynx 
(e.g., common cold, sinusitis, 
pharyngitis).
• Lower Respiratory Tract 
Infection (LRTI): Involves 
trachea, bronchi, lungs (e.g., 
bronchitis, pneumonia).

Causes
Viruses Bacteria
Fungi
Others: Allergens and pollutants

Prevention
Regular handwashing with soap and water
Cover mouth and nose while coughing or sneezing.
Avoid close contact with infected individuals.
Use masks during outbreaks.

• Avoid smoking and exposure to pollutants.
• Vaccinations (e.g., influenza vaccine).
• Maintain good nutrition and hydration.

Treatment
Bacterial 
infections: 
Appropriate 
antibiotics 
Viral infections: 
Mainly supportive 
care (rest, fluids, 
antipyretics)
Symptomatic 
treatment:
o Antipyretics
o Cough suppressants
Oxygen therapy 
Hospitalization: 
For severe 
infections
