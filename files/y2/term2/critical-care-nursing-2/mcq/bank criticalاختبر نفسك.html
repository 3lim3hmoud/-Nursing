Hypertensive Crisis
1
Mention 4 points for patient education about control of blood pressure.
Model Answer
  • Take antihypertensive medications regularly and never stop without consulting the doctor
  • Follow a low-sodium (low-salt) diet and maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly, quit smoking, and reduce alcohol intake
  • Monitor blood pressure at home and attend regular follow-up appointments
2
Mention 4 nursing diagnoses for a patient with hypertensive crisis.
Model Answer
  • Acute pain (severe headache) related to increased arterial pressure
  • Risk for decreased cardiac output related to elevated afterload
  • Ineffective tissue perfusion (cerebral/renal) related to markedly elevated BP
  • Deficient knowledge related to management of hypertension
3
Mention 4 nursing management steps for patients with hypertensive urgency.
Model Answer
  • Monitor blood pressure every 15–30 minutes and document trends
  • Administer oral antihypertensive medications as prescribed
  • Keep the patient in a calm, quiet environment and reduce anxiety
  • Educate patient about medication compliance and lifestyle modifications
DIC
4
Mention 4 nursing interventions for patients with DIC.
Model Answer
  • Monitor vital signs and assess for signs of bleeding (petechiae, purpura, oozing from IV sites)
  • Administer blood products (FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate) as ordered and monitor response
  • Assess all body systems for signs of organ failure due to microthrombi
  • Minimize invasive procedures to reduce bleeding risk; apply pressure to puncture sites
5
Mention 4 precautions to prevent bleeding in DIC patients.
Model Answer
  • Avoid unnecessary injections and invasive procedures; use smallest needle possible
  • Apply gentle pressure for at least 5 minutes after any puncture site
  • Prevent falls and trauma — pad side rails, keep environment safe
  • Use soft toothbrush, avoid rectal temperatures, and use electric razor instead of blades
6
Mention 4 nursing interventions to enhance tissue perfusion in DIC patients.
Model Answer
  • Administer oxygen therapy to maintain adequate SpO₂ and prevent hypoxia
  • Monitor urine output hourly — report output <30 mL/hr (sign of renal hypoperfusion)
  • Assess peripheral pulses, capillary refill, and skin color/temperature
  • Reposition patient every 2 hours and apply sequential compression devices to promote circulation
Stroke
7
Mention 4 diagnostic tests for stroke patients.
Model Answer
  • CT scan (first-line: distinguishes ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke)
  • MRI (more detailed brain imaging)
  • ECG (to detect atrial fibrillation as a cardioembolic source)
  • Blood glucose level (to rule out hypoglycemia mimicking stroke)
8
Mention 4 nursing care priorities for patients with acute stroke.
Model Answer
  • Maintain patent airway and adequate oxygenation; suction secretions as needed
  • Monitor neurological status frequently using GCS and pupil assessment
  • Maintain bed rest with head elevated 30° to reduce ICP
  • Prevent complications: turn patient every 2 hours, early physiotherapy, DVT prophylaxis
9
Mention 4 nursing care priorities during tPA administration in acute stroke.
Model Answer
  • Monitor blood pressure every 15 minutes during infusion (target <185/110 mmHg)
  • Observe closely for signs of bleeding: neurological deterioration, petechiae, GI/urinary bleeding
  • Avoid invasive procedures during and 24 hours after tPA infusion
  • Reassess neurological status every 15–30 minutes for improvement or deterioration
Myasthenia Gravis
10
Mention 4 diagnostic tests for myasthenia gravis.
Model Answer
  • EMG (Electromyography) — shows decremental response on repetitive nerve stimulation
  • Tensilon (Edrophonium) test — brief improvement in muscle strength after IV injection
  • AChR antibody blood test (acetylcholine receptor antibodies)
  • CT scan of chest — to detect thymoma (thymus gland enlargement)
11
Mention 4 teaching tips for patients with myasthenia gravis.
Model Answer
  • Take anticholinesterase medications on time — never skip or delay doses
  • Plan activities during peak medication effect (when strength is best), rest before meals
  • Avoid extreme heat, infections, and emotional stress — these can trigger a crisis
  • Know signs of myasthenic crisis (difficulty breathing/swallowing) and seek emergency care immediately
12
Mention 4 adverse effects and signs of toxicity of anticholinesterase drugs.
Model Answer
  • Excessive salivation, lacrimation, and sweating (cholinergic excess)
  • Abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea
  • Bradycardia and hypotension
  • Increased muscle weakness (cholinergic crisis) — the most dangerous sign; may mimic myasthenic crisis
Seizures
13
Mention 4 causes of seizures.
Model Answer
  • CNS infections (meningitis, encephalitis)
  • Stroke or traumatic brain injury
  • Electrolyte imbalances (hyponatremia, hypoglycemia)
  • Fever (especially febrile seizures in children) and brain tumors
14
Mention 4 nursing interventions during a seizure.
Model Answer
  • Protect the patient from injury — clear the area, pad side rails, never restrain limbs
  • Turn patient on their side to prevent aspiration of secretions
  • Maintain airway patency; do NOT insert anything into the mouth
  • Time the seizure, note type of movements, and call for help; administer prescribed anticonvulsants
15
Mention 4 nursing interventions during the postictal period.
Model Answer
  • Reassess level of consciousness, vital signs, and neurological status
  • Maintain a safe, quiet environment — patient may be confused, drowsy, or combative
  • Reorient the patient calmly; provide reassurance and explain what happened
  • Document duration of seizure, postictal behavior, and report any new deficits to physician
Increased ICP
16
Mention 4 diagnostic tests to determine increased ICP.
Model Answer
  • CT scan (first choice to identify cause and herniation)
  • MRI (detailed brain tissue evaluation)
  • ICP monitoring device (intraventricular catheter — gold standard for direct measurement)
  • Cerebral oxygenation monitoring (intrajugular catheter/brain tissue O₂ probe)
17
Mention 4 nursing precautions for patients with increased ICP.
Model Answer
  • Elevate head of bed to 30° with neck in neutral alignment (avoid neck flexion)
  • Minimize suctioning — limit to <10 seconds; avoid excessive stimulation
  • Monitor neurological status hourly (GCS, pupils, motor response)
  • Avoid activities that raise ICP: Valsalva maneuver, coughing, clustering care without rest periods
18
Mention 4 medical management strategies for patients with increased ICP.
Model Answer
  • Osmotic diuretics: Mannitol IV to draw fluid out of brain tissue
  • Controlled hyperventilation (short-term) to reduce PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduces ICP
  • Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) for vasogenic edema (e.g., around tumors)
  • Surgical intervention: ventricular drainage (EVD) or decompressive craniectomy
Craniotomy
19
Mention 4 indications for craniotomy.
Model Answer
  • Brain tumor (resection)
  • Intracranial hemorrhage or hematoma evacuation
  • Cerebral aneurysm clipping
  • Epilepsy surgery or abscess drainage
20
Mention 4 preoperative care priorities before craniotomy.
Model Answer
  • Complete neurological baseline assessment (GCS, pupils, motor/sensory function)
  • Obtain informed consent; explain procedure and expected outcomes to patient and family
  • Administer prescribed preoperative medications (anticonvulsants, corticosteroids, antibiotics)
  • Prepare the scalp (hair removal if required) and ensure IV access; NPO status per protocol
21
Mention 4 complications after craniotomy.
Model Answer
  • Increased ICP or cerebral edema
  • Infection (meningitis, wound infection)
  • CSF leakage (rhinorrhea or otorrhea)
  • Seizures and neurological deficits (hemiplegia, aphasia)
Bacterial Meningitis
22
Mention 4 nursing diagnoses for patients with bacterial meningitis.
Model Answer
  • Ineffective tissue perfusion (cerebral) related to cerebral edema
  • Hyperthermia related to infectious process
  • Acute pain (severe headache, photophobia) related to meningeal irritation and increased ICP
  • Disturbed sensory perception related to decreased level of consciousness
23
Mention 4 nursing interventions for patients with bacterial meningitis.
Model Answer
  • Administer IV antibiotics as prescribed — early initiation is critical for survival
  • Maintain droplet isolation precautions until 24 hours after antibiotic initiation
  • Monitor neurological status, vital signs, and signs of increased ICP frequently
  • Maintain quiet, darkened room; reduce stimulation; manage fever with antipyretics and tepid sponging
24
Mention 4 complications of bacterial meningitis.
Model Answer
  • Brain herniation due to severely elevated ICP
  • Hearing loss and other cranial nerve damage
  • Seizures and hemiparesis
  • Septicemia, DIC, and septic shock (Waterhouse–Friderichsen syndrome)
Oral Exam Questions
25
What are the causes of thyroid crisis (thyroid storm)?
Model Answer
  • Infection or sepsis
  • Surgery or trauma (especially thyroid surgery)
  • Stopping antithyroid medications suddenly
  • Critical illness, Graves' disease, or amiodarone use
26
What are the indications for enteral nutrition?
Model Answer
  • Functional GIT but inability to take oral food (e.g., severe dysphagia, coma)
  • Major burns or trauma with increased metabolic demands
  • Liver or kidney failure with malnutrition
  • Malnutrition with inadequate oral intake despite functional gut
27
What are the causes of stroke?
Model Answer
  • Ischemic stroke: thrombus or embolus blocking a cerebral artery
  • Hemorrhagic stroke: hypertension causing vessel rupture
  • Ruptured cerebral aneurysm
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM) rupture
28
What is the definition of DIC?
Model Answer

DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation) is a life-threatening disorder in which widespread activation of the clotting cascade leads to the simultaneous formation of microthrombi throughout small blood vessels AND consumption of clotting factors, causing paradoxical severe bleeding.

29
What are the complications of enteral nutrition?
Model Answer
  • Pulmonary aspiration (most serious)
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or constipation
  • Hyperglycemia and electrolyte imbalances
  • Tube displacement, occlusion, or dehydration
30
What are the causes of malnutrition?
Model Answer
  • Inadequate nutrient intake or poor absorption
  • Increased metabolic demands (burns, trauma, sepsis)
  • Chronic diarrhea or GI malabsorption disorders
  • Major surgery or chronic disease (cancer, kidney disease)
31
What are the causes of hypertension?
Model Answer
  • Obesity and high sodium intake
  • Smoking and chronic stress
  • Renal disease and pregnancy complications
  • Withdrawal of antihypertensive medications; sedentary lifestyle
32
What are the causes of DKA?
Model Answer
  • Acute infection (most common trigger)
  • Missing insulin doses or inadequate insulin therapy
  • Surgery, trauma, or physical stress
  • Starvation or newly diagnosed uncontrolled Type 1 diabetes
33
What are the causes of diabetes mellitus?
Model Answer
  • Type 1: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells → no insulin production
  • Type 2: Insulin resistance + relative insulin deficiency (linked to obesity, sedentary lifestyle)
  • Long-term corticosteroid therapy (steroid-induced diabetes)
  • Pancreatic disease or genetic syndromes
