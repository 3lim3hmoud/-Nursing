Definition of Liver Cirrhosis
 Chronic progressive
disease characterized by
fibrosis (scarring) and
nodule formation that
replace normal liver tissue
and disrupts the structure
and function of the liver.

Compensated Decompensated 
• There are still enough 
healthy cells in the liver to 
do its function
• liver can compensate the 
previous damage
•Fatigue, anorexia, weight 
loss , hepatomegaly or 
splenomegaly..
• Failure of the liver 
function to synthesize 
protein and clotting 
factors.

Assessment and diagnostic studies :
❖Presence of manifestation . 
❖lab investigations:
Elevated Ast, Alt 
Elevated serum ammonia level
Hyperbilirubinemia
Prolonged prothrombin time .
Low blood urea nitrogen level
❖ Abdominal ultrasound : enlarged liver.
❖ CT, MRI : give information about the liver size, blood flow and 
obstruction
❖ Liver biopsy

Interferon: for management of viral hepatitis B and C.
corticosteroids : for management of autoimmune hepatitis
Vitamin E :to decrease damage in cirrhotic livers and 
reduce immune abnormalities that contribute to the 
development of the disease.
vitamin K: for management of coagulation disorders 
L-carnitine injections: to improve circulation to the liver in 
people with cirrhosis
Medical management

Nursing Interventions
➢Permit the liver to reestablish its functional ability, 
reduces the demands on the liver and increases the liver's 
blood supply
➢Fowler position for maximal respiratory efficiency.
➢ Oxygen therapy to oxygenate the damaged cells and 
prevent further cell destruction
1- Promoting rest

➢ The patient with cirrhosis who has no ascites or edema 
can receive a high-protein diet if tolerated, supplemented 
by vitamins B complex ,A, C, K. and folic acid).
➢In advance stage of cirrhosis, patient should receive low 
protein, high-caloric diet. 
➢Low sodium diet 
➢low Fat diet
➢Frequent meals are tolerated well than three large meals 
2- Improving Nutritional Status:

• Frequently change of  position to prevent pressure 
ulcers.
• Avoid irritating soaps and the use of adhesive tape to 
prevent trauma to the skin. 
• Minimize scratching by the patient.
3- Providing Skin Care:

•  Fall prevention
• Raised bed side rails 
• A soft toothbrush 
• Apply pressure to all venipuncture sites
• Remove sharp instruments from beside patient
• Frequent monitoring of vital signs and laboratory 
investigations  
4- Reducing risk of injury:

•  Adequate nutrition
• Period of rest
•  Avoid hepatotoxic substances. 
• Refer the client to the appropriate agency or support 
group for assistance with alcohol cessation
• Regular checkups and blood tests to follow the 
progress of the disease
5- Teaching Patients Self-Care

Esophageal varices are 
O Abnormally enlarged 
veins in the lower 
esophagus, usually 
caused liver cirrhosis 
and high blood 
pressure in the liver 
vein (portal 
hypertension)

Manifestation 
• Vomiting blood (hematemesis)
• Black, tarry stools (melena)
• Lightheadedness or shock (in severe 
bleeding

Diagnosis:
• Upper endoscopy (EGD) is the 
gold standard.
• Imaging like ultrasound with 
Doppler, CT, or MRI may also 
show portal hypertension.
• Blood tests may show liver 
dysfunction (e.g., high bilirubin, 
low platelets).

Nursing interventions 
• Monitor vital signs every 15–30 minutes
• Maintain NPO status until cleared
• Establish large-bore IV access for fluids/blood 
transfusion
• Administer blood products as ordered
• Position patient in high Fowler's or side-lying to reduce 
aspiration risk

• Administer medications:
• vasopressin to reduce portal pressure
• Lactulose if hepatic encephalopathy is present
• Prepare for emergency endoscopy
• Monitor for signs of hypovolemic shock: ↓ BP, ↑ 
HR, cold/clammy skin
